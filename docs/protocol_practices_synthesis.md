# Protocol Practices: A Working Synthesis

**How philosophy, strategy, organisational design, and pedagogy connect in Fabric's work**

*Wolfgang Wopperer, with synthesis support from Claude*
*Working draft – March 2026*

---

## What This Document Does

This document traces a single insight across four levels of Fabric's work: **norms are constructed through committed social practice, from within.** It shows how the philosophical foundations (Hegelian Constructivism, the agency-first ontology), the strategic analysis (collective agency, movement self-modelling), the organisational design (relational protocols), and the pedagogy (consciousness raising, conceptual engineering) are not separate theoretical commitments bolted together, but articulations of the same core idea at different scales and in different registers.

It is a working synthesis, not a finished theory. Its purpose is to orient someone coming into this work and wanting to understand how the pieces fit together – and to serve as a reference point for further development.

## 1. Two Perspectives, One World

### The fundamental distinction

We always inhabit two perspectives on the world, and neither is dispensable.

From a **third-person perspective**, we explain phenomena – including ourselves – as parts of the natural world, subject to causal regularities. This is the perspective of science, of evolutionary biology, of the "scientific image" Wilfrid Sellars described.

From a **first-person perspective**, we articulate our situation as agents who deliberate, commit, and give and ask for reasons. This is the perspective of practice, of normativity, of Sellars's "manifest image."

The two perspectives are complementary: first-person articulations that don't correspond to third-person explanations are fantastical; third-person explanations that aren't grounded in and bounded by first-person articulations are imperialist. Only if we respect both do we find reason.

This distinction originates in Kant but gains its full force through the pragmatist and Hegelian traditions. In metaethics, it clarifies the relationship between expressivism (which explains moral practices from a third-person perspective) and constructivism (which articulates them from within). Neither is complete without the other.

### Why this matters for Fabric

Every level of Fabric's work has both a third-person and a first-person face. The strategic analysis of movement ecosystems is a third-person description. The commitment to interconnectedness is a first-person orientation. The agency-first ontology describes how agents maintain themselves. The protocol architecture creates the normative space in which agents constitute their shared commitments. Keeping these perspectives in view – and understanding their relationship – prevents two characteristic errors: reducing the work to mere coordination (third-person only) or to mere aspiration (first-person only).

## 2. The Agency-First Ontology

### The third-person ground

The agency-first ontology provides Fabric's third-person foundation. Its core claim: the universe consists of agents at every scale maintaining their existence against entropy through information closure – the capacity to model one's relevant environment well enough to persist.

This framework synthesises several converging lines of research:

- **Self-organised criticality** (Per Bak): systems naturally evolve to the edge of chaos, generating fractal patterns without fine-tuning.
- **The Free Energy Principle** (Karl Friston): any persisting system must minimise surprise – must maintain a viable model of its environment.
- **TAME** (Michael Levin): even the simplest systems can be understood as minimal cognitive agents solving problems in their environment.
- **The Humean lens** (applied to Tim Palmer's invariant set hypothesis): what we call "laws" are patterns *expressed* by agents, not rules imposed on them.

The key insight is scale invariance: systems at every scale face the same challenge (resisting entropy) and solve it the same way (maintaining predictive boundaries). This creates self-similar patterns across scales – not by design but by necessity.

### The bridge to the first person

Crucially, the agency-first ontology does not merely sit alongside the first-person perspective. It provides a third-person account of **how the first-person perspective emerges**. The developmental sequence runs: attractors (stable regions of state space that systems tend toward) → goal-directedness (agents navigating toward and maintaining attractors) → normativity (agents that can evaluate and revise their own commitments in the light of reasons).

But – and this is the essential complement – once the first-person perspective *has* emerged, it cannot be reduced back to the third-person description. The practice of giving and asking for reasons is continuous with the basic agency that any system exercises in maintaining its coherence, but it operates in the space of reasons, not the space of causes. The violin-maker's knowledge is continuous with the physics of sound but isn't replaceable by it.

### What this means for collective agency

From this ontology, a collective agent – a movement, a network – is not a metaphor. It is a system that maintains its own information closure: a coherent model of its environment, a problem space it navigates, goals it pursues, boundaries it maintains. The conditions for the emergence of such an agent include implicit or explicit coordination between lower-level agents (individuals, groups, organisations), a shared self-model, and cognitive processes for pursuing goals and navigating the problem space.

This is the third-person description. But it immediately points toward its first-person complement: the self-model cannot be imposed from outside; it must be constructed from within, through the practices of mutual commitment that constitute the collective agent's normative space.

## 3. Hegelian Constructivism

### The first-person ground

Hegelian Constructivism names the specific form that first-person articulation takes when we follow Bob Brandom's reading of Hegel. Its core move: normativity is constituted through social practices of attributing and acknowledging commitments.

In Brandom's inferentialism, to grasp a concept is to know what you commit yourself to by deploying it – what follows from it, what's incompatible with it, what entitles you to it. The "space of reasons" is a space of normative statuses – commitments and entitlements – that agents attribute to one another and undertake themselves. This space is fundamentally **social**: you cannot have commitments without someone to hold you to them, and the process of mutual recognition through which commitments are instituted transforms both parties.

The Hegelian move, beyond Kant and beyond Brandom's more abstract formulations, is to insist that this space of reasons is **concrete and historical**. Norms are not transcendentally given; they are constructed in specific social situations, by specific communities, in response to specific practical problems. The space of reasons is always *someone's* space of reasons, shaped by the material and historical conditions in which it emerged.

### Why "constructivism" and not just "Hegel"

The label matters because it specifies what is being taken from Hegel and what is being left behind. What is taken: the social constitution of normativity through practices of mutual recognition, the historicity and concreteness of the space of reasons, the idea that commitment-structures are inherently dynamic – that they generate tensions that drive their own evolution. What is left behind: Hegel's metaphysics, the totalising system, dialectics as a third-person theory of everything, the teleological closure of Absolute Knowing.

In other words: Hegel is used for exploring the first-person perspective *from within*, not as a third-person theory that explains the world's development from outside. This is a Hegel without teleological closure – constructivism without a predetermined endpoint. The construction is never finished; the collective self-constitution is ongoing.

### The connection to ideology critique

Hegelian Constructivism is not only a theory of how norms are constructed; it is also a tool for examining how existing norms reproduce domination. Sally Haslanger's distinction between epistemic critique (revealing distortion and misrepresentation) and moral critique (addressing the unjust conditions such distortions enable) maps directly onto the two-perspective framework: a naturalised, third-person account reveals the contingency of norms, while a first-person, constructivist account provides the tools for emancipatory articulation – for naming and transforming the commitments that sustain oppressive structures.

This is where the philosophical framework connects directly to Fabric's organisational design.

## 4. Relational Protocols as Constructed Normative Space

### From philosophy to organisational design

Fabric's core design proposal is that the network's structure emerges from relational protocols – freely adopted agreements about how to be in relationship. Membership is defined by adoption. What was previously conceived as a layered organisation becomes a topology of shared commitments.

This proposal is a direct instantiation of Hegelian Constructivism. A protocol is a local social space of reasons: a structure of commitments and entitlements that agents attribute to one another and acknowledge for themselves. When agents adopt a protocol, they are entering what Brandom calls a practice of deontic scorekeeping – tracking each other's commitments, holding each other accountable, revising their understanding of what follows from what. The protocol *is* the normative structure, not a description of one.

Identity is not prior to commitment; it is constituted through it. An organisation doesn't first exist as a fixed entity and then choose to adopt commitments. Adopting the commitments is what makes it a member of this particular relational topology. And – as both Hegel and the protocol document insist – this process of adoption is transformative: entering a structure of genuine mutual recognition changes what you are.

### The three layers as aspects of committed practice

Every Fabric protocol has three layers, and the insistence that they are structurally inseparable is the design's most important feature.

The **interface layer** makes commitments explicit. It is the layer where normative statuses are articulated clearly enough to be publicly attributed, acknowledged, contested, and revised. In Brandom's terms, this is *making it explicit*: taking what is implicit in practice and putting it in a form that can be held to account. This is still a first-person activity – agents articulating their own commitments to one another – but it produces the determinacy that accountability requires.

The **practice layer** is where commitments become practical abilities – knowing-how rather than just knowing-that. Regular, embodied, whole-person engagement makes the interface real. A commitment you can state but not enact is a commitment you haven't genuinely undertaken. The somatic check-ins, the storytelling, the regular disciplines of attention and care – these are not supplementary to the interface; they are what gives it life.

The **orientation layer** is first-person articulation applied reflexively to the protocol itself. It is not a third-person monitoring function checking whether the protocol works from outside. It is the protocol's participants articulating, from within their shared practical standpoint, whether their practice is living up to its own commitments. This is moral inquiry in the Hegelian Constructivist sense – examining the conditions of one's own judging, from within.

**The inseparability requirement** enacts the philosophical insight structurally. Interface without practice is empty formalism. Practice without orientation is blind custom. Orientation without interface has nothing determinate to reflect on. Only the inseparable whole is concrete – only together do the three layers constitute a genuine space of reasons.

### Protocols must evolve

Commitment-structures inherently generate tensions between their explicit content and their actual effects. This is not a design flaw to be minimised but an ontological feature to be worked with. The specific ways in which a protocol falls short of its own aspirations are what generate its next iteration. The built-in review dates and evolution criteria are therefore not just good governance practice; they are structurally necessary. A commitment-structure that doesn't evolve through its own contradictions is dead.

The protocol architecture is itself a learning system. Its first cycle is data, not verdict.

## 5. Consciousness Raising and Conceptual Engineering

### The pedagogical question

The preceding sections describe what protocols are (normative structures constituted through committed social practice) and how they're designed (three inseparable layers). What they don't yet address is **how people develop the capacity to participate** – how the critical and constructive abilities the orientation layer requires actually grow in practice.

This is where Paulo Freire's pedagogy and the practice of conceptual engineering enter the picture, providing the methodology that connects the philosophical architecture to lived practice.

### Consciousness raising as protocol pedagogy

Freire's *conscientização* is a process in which people articulate their own situation from within, developing the concepts they need as they go. The conceptual apparatus isn't delivered from outside and then applied; it emerges from the practice of critical reflection on shared experience. The facilitator doesn't teach a theory of oppression and then invite people to apply it; they create conditions under which participants can name what they already live.

As Rodrigo Nunes emphasises in *Neither Vertical nor Horizontal*, the Freirean insight is that knowledge differentials are always local – relative to a specific problem or situation – not a global partition between those who possess all the true knowledge and those who do not. There is process because there is tension, and there is tension because there is difference. But the differential is revisable, and the pedagogical process is one of mutual transformation, not one-way transmission.

Applied to protocols: the orientation layer's questions – "is this protocol serving life or reproducing separation?" – are not checks applied from a theoretical standpoint above the practice. They are questions that participants develop the capacity to ask *through* the practice itself. The capacity to recognise separation logic isn't a prerequisite for entering a protocol; it develops through the experience of being in one – through the somatic check-ins, the storytelling, the confrontation with asymmetry in real decisions.

The practice layer is therefore the pedagogical ground from which the orientation layer's critical capacity grows. Consciousness raising happens not in a separate training programme but in the doing of the work – in the regular, embodied, whole-person engagement that makes the interface real. This is why the layers must be inseparable: remove the practice layer and you remove the experiential ground from which critical perception develops.

### Conceptual engineering as constructive complement

Consciousness raising is the diagnostic moment: perceiving patterns that haven't yet been named. Conceptual engineering is the constructive complement: forging the concepts, distinctions, and questions that make the perceived patterns articulable.

"Relational protocol" itself is a piece of conceptual engineering – taking a term from computing, diplomacy, and indigenous governance and reworking it into something none of those traditions alone contains. The three-layer structure is conceptual engineering. The Organising from Elsewhere guiding questions ("Which experiences are we enabling?" and "Which narrative are we amplifying?") are conceptual engineering. Even "interconnectedness" as a political rather than merely spiritual concept is conceptual engineering.

Conceptual engineering, understood this way, is itself a first-person, constructivist activity. It is not describing what concepts already mean (third-person analysis) but proposing what they should mean given the practical standpoint being articulated from within. And it is subject to the same norms: the engineered concepts are constructed through social practices of commitment, accountable to the community that uses them, and evolve through the dialectic of adoption and critique.

### A first-person methodology for the orientation layer

Putting consciousness raising and conceptual engineering together yields a concrete methodology for the orientation layer:

**The diagnostic moment** (consciousness raising): facilitated inquiry in which protocol participants articulate, from within their shared practice, what patterns of separation they are enacting. The facilitator's role is Freirean – creating conditions for naming, not supplying the names. The somatic and narrative elements of the practice layer provide the perceptual ground from which this naming becomes possible.

**The constructive moment** (conceptual engineering): when existing concepts don't capture what has been perceived, the group develops new ones – new distinctions, new questions, new metaphors. These become candidates for integration into the protocol itself: revising the interface layer, enriching the practice layer, sharpening the orientation questions for the next cycle.

**The integrative moment** (protocol evolution): the revised protocol is adopted through consent, with built-in review dates. This closes the loop: the orientation layer's reflexive inquiry drives protocol evolution, which changes the practice, which generates new experience, which becomes the ground for the next round of consciousness raising.

This cycle is not an implementation detail. It is the protocol architecture's theory of change made operational.

## 6. MEC's Role: From Practice to Pattern

### The movement-wide scale

The Movement Ecology Collective operates at the scale of the movement ecosystem, helping organisations and coalitions develop collective sensemaking capacity, strategic coordination, and the conditions for collective agency to emerge. Its core activities – facilitation, training, movement mapping – are the infrastructure through which the movement's self-model develops.

MEC's strategic analysis provides the bridge between the philosophical foundations and the organisational design. The collective agency article articulates, in strategic language for movement practitioners, the same insight the philosophical framework establishes abstractly: that collective power requires more than the aggregation of individual capacities; it requires a coherent collective agent with its own self-model, its own problem space, and its own cognitive processes. And that this agent needs a self-model based on interconnectedness – one that respects limitations, maintains feedback loops, and refrains from exploitation.

### The three strands as a programme

MEC's current programme – as articulated in a proposal to the Lipman-Miliband Trust – organises its work into three interconnected strands that map directly onto the synthesis's architecture.

**Collective situational analysis** is consciousness raising in the Freirean sense. Participatory political education helps people understand the systemic roots of the crises they are responding to – ecological breakdown, inequality, the rise of the far right – and how these are interconnected. Crucially, this is not an exercise in closing a knowledge gap but in raising consciousness: MEC does not teach a predetermined analysis but supports participants to develop critical understanding from their own lived experience and specific positions. Through collective inquiry using complexity science and political economy, participants move toward a structural analysis that reveals the necessity of fundamental system change. The programme attends carefully to the psychological dimensions of this process – recognising complicity in oppressive structures, or the inadequacy of approaches one has invested in, is challenging work that requires relational trust, skilled facilitation, and an ethic of care.

This is the diagnostic moment described in §5, enacted at movement scale. The practice layer (relational trust, embodied facilitation) provides the ground from which the orientation-layer capacity (structural analysis, recognition of separation logic) can grow.

**Strategy exchange** spaces address a critical gap: people working within different strategic frameworks – community organising, direct action, cooperativism, mutual aid – rarely engage with each other at the level of deep strategic reasoning. The strategy exchange creates conditions for genuine mutual understanding, constructive critique, and identification of complementarities between different approaches. These spaces are designed not for debate but for exchange – building the trust and shared orientation needed for joint action.

An illustrative example: in 2025, MEC facilitated a process at The World Transformed festival in Manchester that brought together several factions within the democratic socialist left for structured deliberation. The result was a shared political platform – an outcome widely regarded as unprecedented, demonstrating that left fragmentation is not inevitable but a product of political culture that can be changed through intentional design of relational space. Similar work with community organisers in Hull combined participatory situational analysis with hegemonic mapping applied to local conditions, modelling how this approach translates across contexts.

**Movement culture building** develops the collaborative infrastructure that sustains collective power over time. From the relationships and practices built in the first two strands, MEC cultivates a growing community of practice: training facilitators, producing and disseminating resources, and spreading practices of collaboration beyond immediate networks. This strand is where consciousness raising and conceptual engineering begin to sediment into durable patterns – the early forms of what may eventually become formalised protocols.

### The trajectory: facilitation → patterns → protocols

MEC's approach follows the methodology the "Perspectives" essay describes for any practice's self-development. You don't start with the theory and derive the practice; you start with the practice, articulate it as you go, and gradually develop the theory from within.

The three strands of the programme embody successive moments in this trajectory:

**Starting with concrete facilitation** (situational analysis, strategy exchange). MEC begins by facilitating on the ground – running formats, convening workshops, supporting organisations and coalitions through real strategic challenges. The TWT and Hull examples are instances of this: each is a specific facilitation in a specific context, producing outcomes that matter to the participants. This is the experiential base from which everything else develops.

**Recognising patterns** (across facilitations, over time). Through repeated practice, patterns emerge – recurring dynamics, effective formats, characteristic failure modes. The discovery that left fragmentation is a *product of political culture* rather than an inevitable feature of political difference is itself a pattern recognised through practice. These patterns are articulated not as abstract theory but as practitioner knowledge: what works, what doesn't, what the conditions for success are. This is first-person articulation in its most basic form.

**Abstracting into protocols** (movement culture building → protocol formalisation). The patterns, once articulated, become candidates for formalisation as protocols – with interface, practice, and orientation layers. A strategy exchange format that has been tested and refined across multiple contexts could become an interface-layer protocol: explicit agreements about how participants enter the space, what commitments they make, how disagreements are held. The facilitation practices that make the format work become the practice layer. The inquiry into whether the exchange is actually building collective power or merely performing collaboration becomes the orientation layer. This formalisation makes the practice adoptable by others who weren't present for the original facilitation, creating the interoperability that enables network-wide coordination without centralised control.

This trajectory is itself a constructivist process happening in real time. The norms that eventually govern the protocols don't pre-exist the practices from which they emerge. They are constructed through the practices of facilitation, reflection, and collective sense-making – made explicit from commitments that were implicit in the practice all along. The Freirean point applies here too: MEC doesn't stand above Fabric providing a meta-layer of training. It is inside the practice, doing the work, and the pedagogical abstraction – the consciousness-raising capacity that eventually enables others to facilitate – emerges from that embedded experience.

Knowledge differentials are local and revisable. MEC knows things that others in the network don't yet know, but that differential closes as people participate in the practice, and it will be replaced by new differentials as new challenges emerge that require new conceptual engineering.

## 7. The Complete Architecture

### How the levels connect

The six preceding sections describe a single architecture viewed from different angles. Here is how they connect:

The **agency-first ontology** (§2) provides the third-person description: agents at every scale maintain information closure, and the patterns they express are what we call regularities. It also provides a third-person account of how the first-person perspective *emerges* – from attractors through goal-directedness to normativity – without claiming to *replace* the first-person perspective once it has emerged.

**Hegelian Constructivism** (§3) provides the first-person complement: norms are constructed through social practices of mutual commitment in a concrete, historical space of reasons. It also provides the tools for ideology critique – for examining whether existing normative structures serve life or reproduce domination.

The **strategic analysis** (§6, drawing on MEC's published work) translates these foundations into a diagnosis and a proposal: the collective agent that movements need doesn't yet exist; it must emerge through coordination, shared self-modelling, and culture change oriented toward interconnectedness.

The **relational protocol architecture** (§4) provides the organisational design: three-layer protocols, freely adopted, structurally inseparable, constituting a topology of shared commitments from which collective agency can emerge. This is Hegelian Constructivism made organisational.

**Consciousness raising and conceptual engineering** (§5) provide the pedagogy: the methodology through which people develop the capacity to participate in and evolve the protocol architecture. This is Freirean consciousness raising embedded in infrastructure – ideology critique as a structural feature of collective practice.

**MEC's trajectory** (§6) provides the developmental pathway: from concrete facilitation through pattern recognition to protocol formalisation, modelling in practice the constructivist process the philosophy describes.

### The reflexive loop

The architecture is not a hierarchy with philosophy at the top and practice at the bottom. It is a loop:

Practice generates experience → consciousness raising names patterns in that experience → conceptual engineering develops the concepts to hold those patterns → protocol evolution formalises the concepts into commitments → the commitments reshape the practice → the reshaped practice generates new experience → and the cycle continues.

At every point in this loop, both perspectives are active. From the third person: the network is an agent maintaining information closure, and the loop is its learning cycle – how it updates its self-model in response to environmental feedback. From the first person: the network is a community of practice constructing its own norms through mutual commitment, and the loop is its process of collective self-constitution.

Neither description is complete without the other. Neither reduces to the other. Together, they provide the theoretical ground for a practice that is both strategically effective (it builds real collective power) and normatively grounded (it serves life rather than reproducing separation).

## Open Questions

This synthesis consolidates what has been developed so far. Several questions remain open and are flagged here for future work:

**The naming question.** "Relational protocol" is conceptual engineering, but does it land with the people who need to adopt it? The same question applies to the three-layer terminology and to "consciousness raising" as a label for what happens in the orientation layer.

**The scale question.** The architecture describes dynamics at the network scale. How do they connect to the neighbourhood scale (assemblies, door-to-door organising) and the movement-wide scale (cross-movement coordination)? The agency-first ontology suggests self-similar patterns across scales, but the specific forms will differ.

**The relationship between MEC and Fabric.** This synthesis positions MEC as operating at the movement-wide scale while Fabric's protocols operate at the network scale. But the two are entangled – MEC's facilitation work feeds the patterns from which Fabric's protocols emerge, and Fabric's protocol experiments generate the experience from which MEC abstracts. The precise shape of this relationship is still developing.

**The decolonial dimension.** The framework draws on Hegelian and pragmatist traditions that are themselves products of European modernity. The protocol architecture explicitly integrates decolonial perspectives (Machado de Oliveira, Sarri Bater's feedback, indigenous governance traditions). How these sit together – whether the framework can genuinely serve decolonial ends or whether it needs more fundamental reworking – is a live question that requires ongoing engagement with people positioned differently from those who developed it.

**The embodiment gap.** The practice layer is where commitments become embodied, and the ZRM analysis suggests specific techniques for bridging intention and action. But the extension of individual somatic practices into genuinely collective somatic intelligence remains speculative. The question is not whether this is possible but what forms it takes and how to develop them.
