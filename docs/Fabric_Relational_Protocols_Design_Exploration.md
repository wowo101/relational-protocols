# Fabric as a Network of Relational Protocols

**An Exploratory Design Document**

**Authors:** Wolfgang Wopperer, with synthesis support from Claude
**Status:** Working draft for team feedback
**Date:** March 2026

---

## 1. Where This Document Sits

This document explores a fundamental reframing of Fabric's organisational design: from *systems mapped onto network layers* to *a network of relational protocols that agents freely adopt*. It emerges from three convergent sources:

The **original living systems design** by Wolfgang and Linda – four systems (resource distribution, decision-making, feedback and learning, conflict) structured across five network layers (wider network, core network, working groups, core team, elders council).

The **Session 08 critique** from the wider design team – which identified the design's language, metaphors, and embedded narratives as reproducing the very paradigm of separation, domination, and control that Fabric exists to move beyond.

The **relational protocols proposal** developed in ongoing incubation-team reflection – that Fabric is best understood not as a layered organisation but as a network of relationships governed by relational protocols, where membership, roles, and structure emerge from the protocols agents choose to adopt.

The document synthesises these sources alongside Ted Rau's *Collective Power*, research into Sociocracy 3.0 (S3), the Meta-Relationality Institute's decolonial framing of "relational protocols," and feedback from Sarri Bater (OpenEdge) whose critique of the original mission document identified structural gaps around racialisation, asymmetry, and the distinction between coordination and genuine interdependence. It is exploratory, not prescriptive. The aim is to think through what a protocol-based architecture could look like, what it would gain and what it would risk, and where it connects to the work already done.

## 2. The Critique and the Seed

### What Session 08 Revealed

The group's feedback on the initial design principles converged on a single deep pattern: the design language – institutions, inclusivity, scaling, leverage, centralise – belongs to the paradigm of modernity, capitalism, and separation. The verbs are mechanistic; the concepts carry colonial and neoliberal weight. The "living" is missing from the living systems.

Several distinct threads emerged:

**Narrative without counter-narrative.** The principles are entirely constructive – build, ensure, design, balance – without any corresponding commitment to interrupting patterns of domination and burden. Liz named this directly: construction without dismantling.

**Concentric circles reproduce centre-periphery power.** The layered network model – with the core team at the centre and the wider network at the periphery – re-creates exactly the power topology Fabric is trying to transcend. Gully and Niki both identified this: the model maps proximity-to-power rather than relationship-to-purpose.

**Missing culture.** Gully drew a crucial distinction between the *system for feedback* (process) and the *culture for feedback* (spaciousness). The design addresses the former but barely touches the latter – and the latter is arguably more important.

**Premature abstraction.** Wolfgang himself named the risk of "premature generalisation" – designing at a level of abstraction that outpaces the actual relationships and experiences the design is meant to serve.

### The Core Proposal

This document proposes a fundamentally different architecture:

**Fabric is a network of relationships**, not a layered organisation. The network's structure is not designed top-down but emerges from the relationships its members form and the commitments they make to each other.

**Relational protocols** are the basic unit of design. A protocol is a freely adopted agreement about how to be in relationship – how to make decisions together, how to share resources, how to hold conflict, how to learn. Protocols are atomic: as small and self-contained as possible, though some naturally cluster.

**Membership is defined by adoption.** You are "in" a particular part of the network by virtue of the protocols you've adopted. What was previously called "core network" becomes the set of people and organisations who have adopted a particular cluster of protocols (e.g., consent-based decision-making + a resource-sharing commitment + a conflict engagement commitment). The layers dissolve; what remains is a topology of shared commitments.

**Agency in relationship is the theory of change.** People are changed by the relationships they are in, and they change those relationships. Adopting a protocol is an act of agency; living it transforms both the adopter and the relationship. This is how collective agency emerges – not through coordination from above but through the accumulation and interconnection of committed relationships.

### The Connection Between Critique and Proposal

The protocol reframing directly addresses the deepest concerns from Session 08:

The **language shifts** from institutional to relational. We no longer "build institutions" or "centralise facilitation" – we invite the adoption of shared commitments. The verbs change: adopt, practise, sense, respond, compost, weave.

The **topology shifts** from concentric circles to an ecosystem. Different protocols are different species in a shared habitat. Some are perennial (like mycorrhizal networks underground); others are seasonal or situational. There is no centre and periphery – only density and sparsity of connection.

The **theory of power shifts**. In the layered model, power flows from position in the hierarchy. In the protocol model, power emerges from the quality and density of relational commitments. This is closer to what Michael Levin describes in biological systems: information existing in relationships rather than in discrete locations.

But there is also a tension that needs naming. The protocol model must somehow hold both radical openness (anyone can adopt) and coherent identity (not everything counts as Fabric). This is the governance question, and it is where the deepest design work remains to be done.

## 3. Protocol as Conceptual Metaphor

Stu introduced the concept of protocol at Session 08, noting that it draws from two traditions: the technological (how machines relate to each other) and the ceremonial (how beings move in shared space). The Meta-Relationality Institute adds a third register. Each illuminates something different – and together, they define the three layers that make a Fabric protocol what it is.

### The Technological Register

In computing, a protocol is an agreed-upon set of rules that enables communication between autonomous entities without requiring a central controller. TCP/IP, HTTP, SMTP – these are agreements about *how to relate* that make the internet possible. Key properties:

**Interoperability without uniformity.** Protocols don't require participating systems to be the same – only to follow the same rules of engagement at the interface.

**Layering.** Protocols stack: higher-level protocols depend on lower-level ones. This creates a natural architecture where underlying protocols enable emergent, more specific ones.

**Version control and evolution.** Protocols change over time through defined processes. Backwards compatibility matters – you can't simply break existing relationships by changing the rules unilaterally.

**Voluntary adoption with network effects.** No one forces you to use HTTP. But because so many others do, the benefits of adoption are enormous. This creates natural convergence without coercion.

For Fabric, this register suggests: protocols as **interoperability agreements** between diverse, autonomous organisations. Each organisation retains its own internal culture, structure, and purpose – but at the interface where it meets other Fabric members, it follows shared rules. This preserves sovereignty while enabling coordination.

### The Ceremonial Register

In diplomacy, ceremony, and indigenous governance, protocol refers to the ways beings enter shared space with respect and intention. You follow protocol not because a machine requires it but because the relationship requires it. Key properties:

**Relational meaning.** A ceremony of greeting is not "overhead" – it is the relationship being enacted. The protocol *is* the relationship, not merely a tool for managing it.

**Embodied practice.** Ceremonial protocols are not just cognitive agreements but bodily practices – ways of moving, speaking, listening, being present. They engage the whole person, not just the rational mind.

**Contextual sensitivity.** The "right" protocol depends on who is present, what has happened before, what the land and the moment ask for. There is no universal checklist.

**Accountability to something larger.** Ceremonial protocols orient participants toward forces, ancestors, ecosystems, and responsibilities that exceed the immediate interaction. They embed each encounter in a larger web of obligation and care.

For Fabric, this register suggests: protocols as **practices of right relationship**. Adopting a protocol is entering into a practice – something that requires ongoing attention, that changes you, that connects you to something larger than the immediate transaction.

### The Meta-Relational Register

The Meta-Relationality Institute (MRI), emerging from Vanessa Machado de Oliveira's work on *Hospicing Modernity*, uses "relational protocol" in yet another sense – as a **container for being-together-across-difference without requiring consensus, convergence, or ideological alignment**. Their "Clearing the Field" protocol is not a governance process but a structured inquiry that enables groups to hold multiple, intersecting layers of transformation together.

Key properties that speak to Fabric's situation:

**Not consensus but relational coherence.** MRI's protocols explicitly do not aim for agreement. They aim for "the ability to move responsibly together across differences at a time when the stakes of misrecognition, simplification, and fragmentation are increasingly high." This directly addresses the Session 08 tension between Gully's insistence on slowness and Stu's frustration with process – the protocol doesn't resolve the tension but creates conditions where it can be held.

**Orientation before action.** The "Clearing the Field" protocol begins with layers of inquiry – temporal orientation, positionality, strategy, grief – before any question of "what to do." It treats the quality of attention and the relational field as prerequisites for any coordination, not afterthoughts.

**Designing for asymmetry.** MRI's work begins from the assumption that participants are differently positioned – with different exposures, different risks, different temporal horizons. The protocol doesn't pretend equality; it names and holds difference.

**Meta-consequence, not meta-crisis.** MRI reframes the polycrisis as not "something going wrong" but as "a consequence of an imperial system reaching its limits." This orientation matters for Fabric: it shifts the design impulse from fixing toward composting, from building toward metabolising.

For Fabric, this register suggests: protocols as **practices of metabolic intelligence** – shared ways of sensing, processing, and responding that honour the fact that participants are differently positioned, differently exposed, and carry different kinds of knowing. This is the register that most directly addresses the Session 08 critique about missing wisdom, nature, and community in the design language.

### Synthesis: Every Protocol Has Three Layers

These three registers are not competing definitions but three dimensions of a single thing. **A Fabric protocol is only complete when it operates at all three layers simultaneously.** This is the architecture's central structural claim – and the design feature that cannot function unless separation logic is actively interrupted.

**Interface layer** (technological register): the explicit agreement that governs how participants meet each other – the rules of engagement, clear and version-controlled.

**Practice layer** (ceremonial register): the embodied discipline that makes the interface real – what you do regularly, with your whole self, that cultivates the relational capacity the interface depends on.

**Orientation layer** (meta-relational register): the recurring inquiry that keeps the protocol honest – the questions you ask together about whether this protocol is actually serving life or reproducing patterns of separation.

A protocol operating at only one or two layers is not a Fabric protocol. Interface without practice produces bureaucracy – correct process without relational depth. Interface without orientation produces well-coordinated separation – efficient structures that leave patterns of domination intact. Practice without interface produces good intentions without accountability. All three are needed, and adopting a protocol means adopting all three.

This is what distinguishes the relational protocols model from conventional governance design. It means you cannot participate in Fabric's decision-making without also committing to the practices and inquiries that surface what decision-making alone cannot see. **The three layers are the non-optional design feature that structurally requires the interruption of separation logic.**

## 4. Agency in Relationship: A Theory of Change

At the heart of this proposal is the claim: *We are changed by relationships and change the relationships we're in.* This is deceptively simple. Let's unpack what it means and what it demands.

### What "Agency in Relationship" Claims

The claim is ontological, not merely strategic. The basic unit of transformation is not the individual, the organisation, or the network – it is the *relationship*. People don't first change internally and then build better relationships; the relationship itself is the site where change happens.

This connects directly to the mission's emphasis on interconnectedness – not as an intellectual position but as an experienced reality. When you adopt a relational protocol and begin practising it, the practice itself reveals interconnectedness. You don't need to be convinced that everything is connected; you experience it through the quality of your attention and the texture of your commitments.

This also connects to Michael Levin's work on biological systems: information doesn't reside in discrete entities but in the relationships between them. A cell's identity is determined not by its genome alone but by its position in a field of signals – its relationships. Change the relationships, and you change what the entity becomes.

### What This Theory of Change Requires

**Specificity about which relationships.** Not all relationships build collective power. The theory needs to specify what kinds of relationships – characterised by what qualities – produce the transformation Fabric seeks. The Dedications and the seven dimensions may already contain this specification; the OfE framework's question "which experiences are we enabling?" is precisely the right one.

**Mechanisms for how relationship produces change.** If this is to be more than aspiration, there need to be identifiable mechanisms:

*Consent practice changes how people hold power.* The experience of deciding by consent – where objections are gifts that improve proposals, where "good enough for now, safe enough to try" replaces the pursuit of perfection – trains a different relationship to power. As Rau documents in *Collective Power*, people who practise consent over time develop a different felt sense of shared responsibility.

*Conflict engagement changes how people hold difference.* When conflict is treated as information and energy rather than failure, participants develop capacity for what MRI calls "remaining in contact across different perspectives and exposures."

*Resource sharing changes how people hold scarcity.* The experience of participating in needs-based co-budgeting or solidarity contributions changes participants' relationship to scarcity and abundance.

*Story harvesting changes how people hold knowledge.* Sharing stories about network experience and seeing patterns emerge trains distributed sensing – the network learning to see itself.

*Somatic practice changes how people hold uncertainty.* As the ZRM analysis demonstrates, the quality of a decision depends not only on the reasoning behind it but on the alignment between rational consent and embodied motivation. When groups learn to sense their somatic response to a proposal, they develop a collective capacity for discernment that exceeds what analytical reasoning alone can provide.

**Patience about timescale.** Relational transformation is slow. It unfolds at the pace of trust, not at the pace of strategy. A realistic theory of change acknowledges this and designs for it.

### The Scale Problem

The deepest challenge for this theory of change is scaling. If transformation happens through relationship, and relationships are inherently local, bounded, and slow, how does Fabric build collective *power* – which by definition requires coordination at scale?

The protocol architecture offers an answer: what scales is not the relationship itself but the *pattern of relating*. When organisations across the network adopt the same consent protocol, they are not in direct relationship with each other – but they are practising the same discipline, developing the same capacities, and becoming interoperable. The protocol is the mechanism through which relational transformation scales beyond the dyad.

This is the insight behind "chainable relationships": strong relationships of solidarity that can be connected into larger assemblages because they share structural properties. Like cells in an organism that don't all communicate directly but are coordinated through shared chemical protocols.

## 5. Living Systems as Protocol Clusters

The original seven living systems – resource distribution, decision-making, feedback and learning, conflict engagement, information flow, care and support, connection and community – do not disappear in a protocol-based architecture. They become *domains* within which clusters of related protocols operate. Each protocol within a domain has all three layers (interface, practice, orientation) – that is what makes it a Fabric protocol.

The three layers also map onto a crucial distinction that Sarri Bater names: the difference between *coordination* and *interdependence*. Interface layers enable coordination – the essential but insufficient work of aligning autonomous agents. Practice layers cultivate the relational capacities that make genuine interdependence possible. Orientation layers are where the group checks whether it is actually moving from one to the other – or whether it has settled into well-coordinated separation.

### Worked Example: Collective Decision-Making Domain

**Consent-as-default protocol**

*Interface:* When any group makes decisions on behalf of or affecting others in the network, they use consent decision-making: a proposal passes unless someone has a reasoned objection – a belief that it would cause harm to the network's shared purpose. This governs how organisations relate to each other in shared decision spaces, ensuring that no single voice can be overridden but equally that no single preference can block progress.

*Practice:* Before or during a consent round, the facilitator invites a brief somatic check-in: "Notice your body's response to this proposal – where do you feel openness, where do you feel constriction?" Drawing on the ZRM's central insight, this treats rational consent and embodied motivation as different registers. A decision that passes analytically but produces widespread somatic constriction may not carry the energy needed for follow-through. The practice does not give body signals veto power but treats them as information to be woven into the process. Over time, participants develop what the ZRM calls *somatic literacy*: attunement to the felt dimension of collective decisions.

*Orientation:* At each review of this protocol, the group examines: Whose objections have been raised, and whose have not? What does the pattern reveal about who carries the cost of this protocol's operation? What structural conditions make objecting difficult for some people – power differentials, cultural norms around deference, fear of social consequences? This orientation inquiry is not optional; the protocol cannot be renewed without it.

**Mandate clarity protocol**

*Interface:* Every circle, role, or working group articulates what it is responsible for and empowered to decide – and makes this legible to the rest of the network. When one circle's decision affects another's domain, the affected group is consulted. Mandates are set by consent within the broader group, and changing a mandate requires re-engaging those affected.

*Practice:* Image-based strategy sensing, adapted from the ZRM's needs phase: at the start of a strategic process, participants select images based on somatic response rather than analytical judgment, then explore the collective pattern. This bypasses the analytical filtering that tends to reproduce existing narratives and accesses pre-verbal, body-based knowing about what a group's mandate actually *is* – not just what it says on paper.

*Orientation:* Periodically, each circle examines its mandate in relation to power: Has this domain become a site of accumulation? Who benefits from the current boundaries? Whose work falls through the gaps between mandates?

**Open decision records protocol**

*Interface:* All mandates, role holders, and decision records are accessible to anyone in the network. This governs the relationship between those who decide and those affected by decisions, ensuring that authority is always visible.

*Practice:* Shared orientation metaphors, adapted from the ZRM's motto goal process: the network and its circles develop figurative, embodied expressions of shared direction – not analytical targets but images and phrases that speak to the whole person. These keep shared purpose alive as felt commitment rather than abstract mission statement.

*Orientation:* Regular narrative review, adapted from the OfE framework: examine the narratives embedded in our decision-making practices. Are our processes reproducing the paradigm of separation, or enacting interconnectedness?

**Tending shared roles protocol**

*Interface:* Key roles rotate on defined terms with overlap periods where the outgoing and incoming holders work together. This governs the relationship between role-holders and those they serve, preventing the accumulation of informal authority in individuals.

*Practice:* During handover, the outgoing holder shares not only tasks and information but their felt experience of the role – what it cost, what it opened, what they would do differently. This creates a culture of honest reflection about power and burden.

*Orientation:* At each rotation, the group asks: Has this role become a "gravity well" that attracts more authority than intended? What does the pattern of who holds this role reveal about our assumptions?

**Weaving between circles protocol**

*Interface:* At least one member of each circle participates as a full member of a neighbouring circle – not as a "representative" reporting back, but as someone who genuinely holds both relationships simultaneously. This governs how circles stay in contact with each other.

*Practice:* The weaver brings not just information but felt experience between the two spaces – what it's like to sit in each circle, where the energy is, where the tension is.

*Orientation:* The weaver periodically reflects with both circles on what is flowing between them and what is not – and what the blockages reveal about the network's health.

### Worked Example: Conflict Engagement Domain

**Conflict-as-information protocol**

*Interface:* The network treats conflict not as failure but as energy and information about system dynamics. Any member can name a conflict without it being treated as a problem to be solved or a person to be blamed.

*Practice:* When conflict is named, the first response is to notice it somatically – where does it live in the body? What does the collective tension reveal about what's at stake? This slows the rush to resolution and honours the information conflict carries.

*Orientation:* Root-cause inquiry, adapted from transformative justice: when conflict arises, the question is not only "what happened?" but "what conditions made this possible?" The inquiry attends to power differentials, historical patterns, and structural factors.

**Staged support protocol**

*Interface:* A clear pathway that governs how support is offered as conflict deepens: direct conversation → peer support → mediation → structural review. At each stage, the protocol defines who can be called upon and what their role is.

*Practice:* Regular training in empathic listening, de-escalation, and perspective-taking – offered across the network so that support capacity is distributed rather than concentrated in specialists.

*Orientation:* When patterns emerge from mediation that point to systemic rather than interpersonal causes, a structured process generates proposals for adjustment – ensuring that individual conflicts feed back into the network's structural learning.

**Boundary-tending protocol**

*Interface:* The network can, through a defined and transparent process, determine that a member's behaviour is incompatible with continued participation. The process involves multiple rounds of engagement before reaching this point.

*Practice:* The process is held by people trained in both accountability and care – not as a judicial proceeding but as a relational reckoning.

*Orientation:* After any boundary decision, the group examines what the conflict revealed about the network's structures, not only about the individual involved.

### The Other Domains (Principles-Level)

The remaining domains can be developed following the same three-layer architecture:

**Resource Distribution Domain.** Protocols for: solidarity contributions (how resources flow between organisations, with what transparency, governed by what criteria); needs-based co-budgeting (how shared resources are allocated through participatory process); mutual aid (how members call on each other in crisis). Each with practice and orientation layers attending to: whose labour is invisible, whose needs go unmet, and whether resource flows are prefiguring alternatives or reproducing extraction.

**Feedback and Learning Domain.** Protocols for: story harvesting (how experience is shared and sense-made across the network); structural review (how operational learning feeds into governance evolution); team retrospectives (how circles reflect on their own health). Each with practice and orientation layers attending to: whether feedback practices are extractive, whose knowledge counts, and whether the network is learning or merely reporting.

**Information Flow Domain.** Protocols for: transparent communication (how information moves between circles and organisations); shared learning (how insights reach those who need them). Each with practice and orientation layers attending to: what information is being suppressed, who controls narrative, and whose stories get amplified.

**Care and Support Domain.** Protocols for: mutual support (how groups attend to each other's wellbeing); welcoming (how new members enter relationship with the network). Each with practice and orientation layers attending to: who carries the relational weight, who feels at home, and whose care is invisible.

## 6. What S3 Offers (and What Relational Protocols Need Beyond It)

S3 provides a useful pattern library – 74 patterns across ten categories, freely available under Creative Commons. Its modular architecture aligns with the protocol model: patterns can be adopted incrementally, combined in context-specific ways, and evolved through defined processes.

### What Fabric Can Draw From

**The agreement lifecycle.** S3 treats agreements as living documents with built-in review dates, evaluation criteria, and explicit evolution processes. Every Fabric protocol should have: a clear description of the driving need, what is agreed, who is responsible, when it will be reviewed, and how it can be changed.

**Relational building blocks.** Circles, roles, weaving between circles, and mandate clarity are well-tested patterns that Fabric can adopt as interface layers within protocols.

**The driver concept.** S3's insistence that every agreement responds to a specific organisational driver (a situation requiring response) prevents the proliferation of protocols for their own sake and keeps design grounded in actual need.

**Fractal organisation.** S3's pattern for federated networks of semi-autonomous organisations – where functional domains are coordinated through delegate circles while constituent organisations retain sovereignty – is directly relevant to Fabric's inter-organisational context.

### Where Relational Protocols Go Further

**Designing for asymmetry.** S3 assumes rough equivalence between participants. Its consent mechanism privileges analytical and verbal forms of participation. Relational protocols, informed by MRI's orientation, begin from the assumption that participants are *differently positioned*. This means: every protocol design process must include a power analysis; consent processes must be supplemented with practices that surface embodied and intuitive knowing; objection processes must attend to the structural conditions that make objecting difficult for some people.

**The three-layer requirement.** S3 is comprehensive on structural mechanisms but relatively silent on the emotional, relational, and cultural conditions those mechanisms require. The three-layer architecture addresses this by insisting that no interface operates without its corresponding practice and orientation layers.

**Accountability to something larger.** S3's seven principles (effectiveness, consent, empiricism, continuous improvement, equivalence, transparency, accountability) are internally coherent but self-referential – they optimise the system's own functioning without reference to anything beyond it. Fabric's protocols need an additional reference point: the service of life. Every protocol review should ask: "Does this serve the flourishing of life, or only the efficiency of our network?"

## 7. Protocol Governance: Who Decides the Rules?

The protocol model's greatest risk is incoherence – if anyone can propose any protocol and claim it's Fabric, the network loses identity and purpose. There must be governance of the protocols themselves.

### Shared Commitments: The Ground

A small set of commitments that all Fabric protocols must serve. These are not protocols themselves but the ground on which protocols stand. They should be derived from the existing **Dedications** and/or the **seven dimensions**, with the addition of an explicit commitment to naming and interrupting racialisation, colonialism, and extractive domination as foundational structures – not peripheral additions to the polycrisis but central to its construction. For many communities, the social contract was always violent, conditional, and unequally applied. Our protocols must reckon with this as a starting condition, not an afterthought. The precise formulation of these shared commitments is to be developed collectively.

These commitments function like MRI's "relational orientation" – they don't prescribe action but shape the field within which action takes place.

### Protocol Creation and Evolution

New protocols are proposed in response to identified needs. The design process includes:

An **asymmetry check** (mandatory, not optional): Who benefits from this protocol? Whose ways of knowing does it privilege? What barriers to participation does it create? Who carries the cost of its operation? This check must be completed before a protocol can be ratified.

A **narrative check** (adapted from OfE): What experiences does this protocol enable? What narrative does it amplify?

A **three-layer completeness check**: Does the protocol have a clear interface, a defined practice, and a built-in orientation inquiry? A protocol missing any layer cannot be adopted as a Fabric protocol.

A **consent round** among those who will be affected.

Built-in **review dates** and evolution criteria.

### How Protocols Spread

Protocols are adopted voluntarily. But "voluntarily" doesn't mean "without consequence" – adopting certain clusters of protocols is what constitutes membership in different parts of the network. Adoption creates mutual accountability: if you've committed to a consent protocol, others can hold you to it – including the practice and orientation layers, not just the interface.

### How Protocols Change

Protocols are living agreements. They change through the same consent process by which they were created, with the additional requirement that changes are communicated to all adopters and that transition periods allow adaptation.

### Structural Integrity

A dedicated function – whether an elders council, a protocol stewardship circle, or a rotating review body – periodically examines the protocol ecosystem as a whole. Not to control it but to notice: Are protocols proliferating beyond what the network can sustain? Are they drifting from the shared commitments? Are there gaps – areas of collective life where no protocols exist and people are struggling? Are the orientation layers actually being practised, or have they quietly been dropped? This is the "wisdom" function Gail called for – and it needs to be protected from becoming the "gravity well" that Stu warned about from XR's experience.

## 8. What Changes, What Remains

The protocol reframing does not throw out the existing design work. Most of the concrete mechanisms in the living systems draft – consent-based decision-making, story harvesting, staged conflict support, solidarity contributions – survive intact. What changes is:

**The frame.** From "systems we build" to "practices we adopt." This shifts the locus of agency from designers to participants.

**The topology.** From concentric circles to an ecosystem of shared commitments. The network's shape is not prescribed but emergent – determined by which protocols cluster together in practice.

**The language.** From institutional (build, ensure, leverage, centralise) to relational and ecological (adopt, practise, sense, compost, weave, tend).

**The theory of power.** From position-based (proximity to the core) to practice-based (density and quality of relational commitments).

**The structural requirement.** Every protocol has three layers – interface, practice, orientation – and all three must be adopted together. This is what makes the architecture transformational rather than merely optimising: you cannot coordinate within Fabric without also committing to the embodied practices and critical inquiries that surface what coordination alone cannot see.

What remains is the fundamental insight that collective agency requires structure – that aspiration without structure produces the tyranny of structurelessness. Protocols *are* institutions, in the deepest sense: shared practices that shape behaviour and outlast individual participants. The question is not whether to institutionalise but *what kind* of institutions to tend – and the protocol model's answer is: institutions that are transparent, evolvable, voluntarily adopted, and accountable to the service of life.

## 9. Open Questions

**Minimum viable protocol set.** What is the smallest cluster of protocols that constitutes "being part of Fabric"? This defines the network's boundary – and getting it wrong in either direction (too few → incoherence; too many → exclusion) carries real consequences.

**The naming question.** "Protocol" carries the right connotations in the technological and ceremonial registers but may feel cold or technical to some. The MRI framing of "relational protocol" helps, but the team should test whether this language lands – or whether something else (commitment, practice, agreement, covenant) works better in context.

**The capacity question.** The three-layer architecture is conceptually elegant but practically demanding. Does the network have the facilitation capacity, the emotional literacy, and the time to sustain practice and orientation layers alongside the interface work? How do we keep the three layers alive without them becoming bureaucratic overhead?

**The governance paradox.** Who designs the first protocols? There is an unavoidable bootstrapping problem – the protocol-creating process cannot itself be protocol-governed until protocols exist. How to handle this founding period with integrity, avoiding both paralysis and unaccountable power?

**The ecosystem metaphor.** Gail's suggestion of species-in-a-shared-habitat is generative – but ecosystems include predators, parasites, and die-offs. How far does the metaphor serve, and where does it mislead?

**The relationship to existing organisations.** Fabric's members are not individuals but organisations with their own cultures, structures, and power dynamics. Protocols operate at the *interface* between organisations, not within them. How to handle the gap between what an organisation commits to at the interface and how it actually operates internally?

**Positionality.** This document is written primarily from one position within the network. Whose experiences and concerns is it most likely to miss? Sarri Bater's feedback has already named several – racialisation, the burden that falls on those carrying historical cost, the gap between transformational aspiration and optimising design. What other positions need to inform the next iteration?

## 10. Suggested Next Steps

1. **Test the framing with the wider design team.** Share this document and gather somatic as well as intellectual responses. Does the protocol language open something, or does it close something down? Does the three-layer model feel workable or overloaded?

2. **Develop the minimum viable protocol cluster.** What interface-practice-orientation bundles must everyone adopt? Start with the smallest possible set and resist the urge to be comprehensive.

3. **Pilot one domain in full.** Choose the domain where the team has most energy and develop a complete set of three-layer protocols as a working prototype. Feedback and learning may be the natural candidate – it is both practically needed and relatively low-stakes.

4. **Apply the OfE check.** For each protocol developed, ask: Which experiences does this enable? Which narrative does it amplify? Does it serve the paradigm we're trying to exit, or the one we're trying to enter?

5. **Engage Sarri and OpenEdge.** Sarri's offered pathway – a bounded, explicit paid role to help articulate and design for racialisation, asymmetry, and burden redistribution – could be invaluable for ensuring the shared commitments and asymmetry checks are not abstract but grounded.

6. **Consult the S3 contact on integration.** The contact who integrates sociocracy with indigenous organising traditions from North America could be invaluable for the "designing for asymmetry" challenge.

7. **Engage with MRI's resources.** The "Clearing the Field" protocol series – particularly the systemic unravelling resource – offers orientation practices that could directly inform Fabric's orientation layers.

---

*This document is offered as a contribution to collective thinking, not as a finished design. It holds questions alongside proposals, and it expects to be changed by the relationships it enters.*
