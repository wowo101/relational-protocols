Protocols are not designed theoretically and then adopted. They emerge from a developmental sequence: **facilitation → pattern recognition → formalisation**. You start with concrete practice – running formats, facilitating real decisions, holding actual conflicts. Over time, patterns emerge: recurring dynamics, effective formats, characteristic failure modes. Only then do you formalise into [[Every protocol has three inseparable layers|three-layer protocols]] – giving the pattern an explicit accord, naming the ritual that makes it work, and building in the compass inquiry that keeps it honest.

A protocol that skips this sequence – designed at a distance and adopted without prior practice – lacks the experiential ground from which the ritual and compass layers draw their life. The norms that govern a protocol don't pre-exist the practices from which they emerge; they are [[Protocols are normative structures|constructed through those practices]], made explicit from commitments that were implicit all along. [[Naming experiences is how protocols learn|Consciousness raising]] is what turns lived experience into articulable pattern, and articulable pattern into adoptable protocol.

The same logic applies at a smaller scale: every protocol version is a hypothesis, not a settlement. Built-in review dates and evolution criteria ensure that each version is tested in practice and revised through the same cycle of experience, naming, and formalisation. The compass layer exists precisely to surface what isn't working – but only if the network actually acts on what it learns.
