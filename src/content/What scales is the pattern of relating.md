title: "What scales is the pattern of relating"

description: "Relational transformation is local and slow – but protocols let the pattern scale beyond the dyad"
---

If transformation happens through relationship, and relationships are inherently local, bounded, and slow, how does Fabric build collective *power* – which by definition requires coordination at scale?

The protocol architecture offers an answer: what scales is not the relationship itself but the *pattern of relating*. When organisations across the network adopt the same consent protocol, they are not in direct relationship with each other – but they are practising the same discipline, developing the same capacities, and becoming interoperable. The protocol is the mechanism through which relational transformation scales beyond the dyad.

This is the insight behind "chainable relationships": strong relationships of solidarity that can be connected into larger assemblages because they share structural properties. Like cells in an organism that don't all communicate directly but are coordinated through shared chemical protocols. Michael Levin's work on biological systems illuminates the analogy: information doesn't reside in discrete entities but in the relationships between them. A cell's identity is determined not by its genome alone but by its position in a field of signals – its relationships. Change the relationships, and you change what the entity becomes.

The implication for design is that protocols must work between strangers, not only between friends. This is what distinguishes a protocol architecture from a community of practice – and it is why [[Agency in relationship builds collective power]] can operate at network scale rather than only within tight-knit groups. At the same time, [[Participating means enacting protocols]] means the network's topology remains emergent: what scales is the shared discipline, not a centrally imposed structure.
