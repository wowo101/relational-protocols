The original seven living systems – resource distribution, decision-making, feedback and learning, conflict engagement, information flow, care and support, connection and community – do not disappear in a protocol-based architecture. They become *domains* within which clusters of related protocols operate. Each protocol within a domain has all three layers ([[Every protocol has three layers|interface, practice, and orientation]]) – that is what makes it a Fabric protocol.

The three layers also map onto a crucial distinction that Sarri Bater names: the difference between *coordination* and *interdependence*. Interface layers enable coordination – the essential but insufficient work of aligning autonomous agents. Practice layers cultivate the relational capacities that make genuine interdependence possible. Orientation layers are where the group checks whether it is actually moving from one to the other – or whether it has settled into well-coordinated separation.
