The [[Every protocol has three layers|three-layer architecture]] describes what protocols are. What it doesn't yet address is how people develop the capacity to participate – how the critical and constructive abilities the orientation layer requires actually grow in practice. This is where Paulo Freire's pedagogy and the practice of conceptual engineering enter the picture.

## Consciousness raising as protocol pedagogy

Freire's *conscientização* is a process in which people articulate their own situation from within, developing the concepts they need as they go. The conceptual apparatus isn't delivered from outside and then applied; it emerges from the practice of critical reflection on shared experience. As Rodrigo Nunes emphasises, knowledge differentials are always local – relative to a specific problem or situation – not a global partition between those who know and those who don't. There is process because there is tension, and there is tension because there is difference. But the differential is revisable.

Applied to protocols: the orientation layer's questions – "is this protocol serving life or reproducing separation?" – are not checks applied from a theoretical standpoint above the practice. They are questions that participants develop the capacity to ask *through* the practice itself. The somatic check-ins, the storytelling, the confrontation with asymmetry in real decisions – these are the experiential ground from which critical perception develops. The practice layer is the pedagogical ground from which the orientation layer's critical capacity grows.

## Conceptual engineering as constructive complement

Consciousness raising is the diagnostic moment: perceiving patterns that haven't yet been named. Conceptual engineering is the constructive complement: forging the concepts, distinctions, and questions that make the perceived patterns articulable. "Relational protocol" itself is a piece of conceptual engineering – taking a term from computing, diplomacy, and indigenous governance and reworking it into something none of those traditions alone contains. So is the three-layer structure. So are the Organising from Elsewhere guiding questions: "Which experiences are we enabling?" and "Which narrative are we amplifying?"

Conceptual engineering is itself a first-person, constructivist activity – proposing what concepts should mean given the practical standpoint being articulated from within. The engineered concepts are constructed through social practices of commitment, accountable to the community that uses them, and evolve through the dialectic of adoption and critique.

## The three-moment methodology

Putting consciousness raising and conceptual engineering together yields a concrete methodology for the orientation layer:

**The diagnostic moment** (consciousness raising): facilitated inquiry in which protocol participants articulate, from within their shared practice, what patterns of separation they are enacting. The facilitator's role is Freirean – creating conditions for naming, not supplying the names.

**The constructive moment** (conceptual engineering): when existing concepts don't capture what has been perceived, the group develops new ones – new distinctions, new questions, new metaphors. These become candidates for integration into the protocol itself.

**The integrative moment** (protocol evolution): the revised protocol is adopted through consent, with built-in review dates. The orientation layer's reflexive inquiry drives protocol evolution, which changes the practice, which generates new experience, which becomes the ground for the next round of consciousness raising.

## The reflexive loop

This cycle is the protocol architecture's theory of change made operational. It connects every level of the [[Norms are constructed through committed practice|philosophical architecture]]: practice generates experience → consciousness raising names patterns → conceptual engineering develops concepts → protocol evolution formalises concepts into commitments → commitments reshape practice → reshaped practice generates new experience. At every point, both perspectives are active: from the third person, the network is an agent updating its self-model; from the first person, the network is a community constructing its own norms through mutual commitment. Together they ground a practice that is both strategically effective and normatively grounded – building real collective power while serving life rather than reproducing separation.
