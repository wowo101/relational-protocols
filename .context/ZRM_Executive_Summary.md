# ZRM and Fabric's Decision-Making Systems – Executive Summary

## The Zürcher Ressourcen Modell

The Zurich Resource Model (ZRM) is a self-management training developed by Maja Storch and Frank Krause at the University of Zurich. It solves a specific problem: the well-documented gap between intending to do something and actually doing it. Its distinctive move is to treat the body's felt responses – not rational analysis alone – as the primary guide for identifying what someone genuinely wants, and then to systematically align cognitive, emotional, and physiological resources behind that intention.

### Theoretical Foundations

The ZRM rests on three pillars:

**The Rubikon Process** (from Heckhausen and Gollwitzer's motivational psychology) maps the journey from vague need to completed action across five phases: Needs → Motive → Intention → Pre-actional Preparation → Action. The crucial insight is that crossing from intention into action requires more than rational commitment – the whole organism needs to be aligned.

**Damasio's somatic markers** provide the navigational instrument. A somatic marker is a body signal – warmth, constriction, butterflies, settling – that functions as an automatic evaluative response, drawing on accumulated experience to bias decisions before conscious deliberation concludes. The ZRM makes these markers central: a goal that doesn't produce a positive somatic response is unlikely to be pursued with full energy, regardless of how rationally compelling it is.

**Dual-process theory** (via Kahneman, LeDoux, Roth) distinguishes the conscious, analytical mind from the fast, holistic, body-based unconscious. The ZRM's power lies in insisting that effective action requires *synchronisation* of both – neither system alone suffices.

### Key Methods

- **Image-based needs identification**: Participants select images based on somatic response, bypassing verbal rationalisation to access what the body already knows about what matters.
- **Motto goals**: Attitude goals expressed in figurative, metaphorical language ("I am a tree with deep roots") rather than SMART targets – designed to speak to the whole person and synchronise conscious and unconscious motivation.
- **Somatic literacy**: Learning to notice and interpret body signals as evaluative information.
- **Embodiment**: Physical practices (posture, movement, gesture) that anchor intentions in the body.
- **Priming**: Environmental cues placed in daily life to maintain activation of the desired state.
- **If-then plans**: Pre-committed responses to anticipated situations, creating what Gollwitzer calls "strategic automaticity" – the behaviour is triggered by the situation rather than requiring effortful self-control.

### Empirical Standing

The ZRM's own evidence base is preliminary but encouraging – notably an RCT showing reduced cortisol stress responses (Storch et al., 2007, *Health Psychology*). Its constituent parts are better evidenced: implementation intentions have strong meta-analytic support (d = 0.65 across 94 studies), the somatic marker hypothesis is broadly accepted in affective neuroscience, and embodied cognition research supports the bidirectional link between body states and psychological experience. More rigorous than most coaching frameworks, but awaiting larger-scale replication as an integrated method.

## Fit with Fabric's Design

The ZRM addresses a genuine gap in Fabric's current design scaffolding. The scaffolding is strong on *structural* mechanisms for collective decision-making – consent processes, distributed authority, escalation protocols – but relatively thin on the *experiential and embodied dimensions* where follow-through lives or dies.

Three specific connections to Fabric's design criteria:

**Enabling agency** (actually doing the agreed things): This is the ZRM's central problem. Somatic marker checking during decision processes can surface misalignment between rational consent and embodied motivation *before* it manifests as lack of follow-through. If-then plans can bridge collective decisions into individual action.

**Incorporating somatic/relational information**: The ZRM provides concrete, learnable techniques for what the design scaffolding's Principle 3.2 ("Honour relational knowing; resist extractive metrics") describes philosophically – and what McGilchrist frames as restoring the "right hemisphere's" broad, embodied, contextual mode of attention alongside analytical cognition.

**Fostering interconnectedness**: The most speculative but potentially most significant application. Sharing somatic responses in a group creates a different quality of connection than sharing opinions. Collective image work and shared metaphor create what Fabric's mission calls "memes of interconnectedness." The body is a site of connection – somatic awareness opens us not only to our own felt sense but to resonance with others.

## Practical Experiments

Six pilots that could be tried within Fabric's existing processes:

1. **Somatic consent check** – after a standard consent round, a brief body check-in to surface information that might otherwise emerge later as passive resistance
2. **Image-based strategy sessions** – using ZRM-style image selection to access collective needs at the start of strategic processes
3. **Collective motto goals** – figurative, embodied expressions of shared orientation developed alongside analytical strategy
4. **Embodiment in meeting rhythms** – brief physical practices as integral information-gathering, not warm-up
5. **Somatic literacy for facilitators** – ZRM-informed training as part of the Facilitator role
6. **If-then plans for network commitments** – supporting participants in forming specific implementation intentions after collective decisions

## The Central Challenge

The ZRM was developed for individual self-management. Extending it to collective decision-making requires creative adaptation – not simply having everyone do individual somatic check-ins simultaneously, but developing what we might call *collective somatic intelligence*: the network's capacity to sense, through embodied relational awareness, what is alive, what is needed, and what wants to happen next.

This is speculative territory, but it sits at the intersection of Fabric's mission (overcoming separation through embodied interconnectedness), its design principles (safe-to-fail experimentation in complex domains), and a theoretically grounded method with practical techniques. The question, in the scaffolding's own language, is not "Will this definitely work?" but "Is it safe enough to try, and can we learn from trying?"
