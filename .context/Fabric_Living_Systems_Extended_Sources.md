## Source Summaries

### 1. Enspiral and Loomio

**Context:** Decentralised network of social enterprises (Enspiral) and consent-based decision-making platform (Loomio), both originating from New Zealand's post-Occupy movement ecosystem. Operating since 2011, with ~15,000 monthly active Loomio users globally.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Collaborative Funding (Cobudget):**
Enspiral pioneered transparent collective resource allocation through Cobudget – an open-source tool where anyone can propose projects ("buckets") and participants distribute allocated funds transparently. This shifts resource decisions from management or grant committees to distributed network members. The model makes visible the normally hidden work of financial allocation.

**Advice Process:**
Rather than seeking consensus, any individual can make any decision provided they: (1) take responsibility for outcomes, and (2) consult those affected or with relevant expertise – then decide independently while remaining accountable. This balances autonomy with input, enabling faster decisions than consensus while maintaining distributed accountability.

**Consent vs Agreement:**
Enspiral explicitly distinguishes between consent (you can live with it) and agreement (it's your first preference), recognising true consensus rarely occurs but productive decisions can proceed with consent. This aligns with sociocratic principles in the main report.

**Conflict Escalation:**
A structured four-stage process: direct conversation → mediation with trained network mediator → broader involvement. Mediation costs are negotiated with involved parties, creating accountability for resolution itself.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **The "dark economy" problem:** Invisible organisational labour leads to burnout and devaluation. Enspiral addressed this through explicit "catalyst" roles.
- **Dunbar's number:** Trust-based governance becomes harder as networks exceed ~150 members. Federation may be necessary at scale.
- **Leadership distribution paradox:** Foundation directors performed substantial unpaid operational work invisible to the network, undermining distributed leadership claims.

**Relevance to Fabric:** The advice process aligns with Fabric's emphasis on distributed decision-making. Cobudget offers a concrete model for the proposed network economy. Conflict escalation structures could inform the "Mediators" role.

### 2. Mondragon Cooperatives

**Context:** World's largest worker-cooperative network, comprising ~80,000 members across 266 businesses in the Basque region of Spain. Founded 1956, operating for nearly 70 years.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Federated Architecture:**
Representation in the Cooperative Congress is weighted inversely to cooperative size – explicitly preventing domination by largest entities. This protects smaller cooperatives' influence within the network.

**Solidarity Redistribution:**
Successful cooperatives contribute 10% of profits to common funds supporting struggling enterprises. A reserve capital fund (FISO) provides emergency financing. This formalised solidarity mechanism distinguishes Mondragon from conventional corporations – when individual enterprises face crisis, the network collectively absorbs costs.

**Wage Equity:**
Each cooperative democratically sets wage ratios between highest and lowest earners, typically 3:1 to 6:1 (compared to 100:1+ in conventional corporations). These remain subject to periodic democratic review.

**Two-Tier Governance:**
General Assemblies (all workers vote on major issues) combined with elected Governing Councils and appointed managers. This balances participation with operational efficiency through representative democracy for strategic decisions and delegated management for operations.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **Internationalisation dilemma:** 168 of 266 affiliates operate as conventional capitalist subsidiaries, creating a "coopitalist" hybrid contradicting core principles.
- **Participation at scale:** Larger cooperatives report workers feeling alienated despite formal voting rights – effective participation requires time, information, and political skill.
- **Solidarity limits:** The 2013 Fagor bankruptcy showed solidarity funds cannot overcome structural market forces affecting entire sectors.

**Relevance to Fabric:** The inverse weighting mechanism directly addresses Fabric's challenge of power imbalances between larger and smaller member organisations. The 10% solidarity contribution offers a model for internal resource flows. The two-tier governance (strategic vs operational) maps onto Fabric's Core/Network distinction.

### 3. Zapatista Autonomous Governance

**Context:** Indigenous-rooted autonomous governance in Chiapas, Mexico since 1994. Operates across thousands of communities with nested decision-making structures. Recently reorganised (2023) after 30 years.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Nested Assemblies:**
Community assemblies (foundation) → Municipal bodies (MAREZ) → Regional Caracoles → Juntas de Buen Gobierno. Authority flows upward from grassroots rather than being imposed from above. The 2023 reorganisation further inverted this, making communities the "principal basis" with hyperlocal Local Autonomous Governments (GAL).

**Mandar Obedeciendo (Lead by Obeying):**
Leaders act as delegates implementing community decisions rather than representatives making decisions on behalf of communities. Mandatory rotation prevents power concentration. Oversight committees monitor power abuse. This directly addresses the "tyranny of structurelessness" identified in the main report.

**Resource Autonomy:**
Recovery of over one million acres provides territorial basis for autonomy. Deliberate rejection of state funding to maintain independence. Cooperative production (coffee, agriculture, artisanry) funds independent healthcare, education, and administration.

**Structural Gender Integration:**
Women participate in assemblies, hold rotated leadership, and lead as commanders. Special women's assemblies address gender-specific concerns alongside integrated participation.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **What sustained 30 years:** Core principles ("everything for everyone, nothing for ourselves"), consistent application of rotation and accountability, material resource base.
- **Adaptive evolution:** The 2023 restructuring demonstrates capacity to fundamentally rethink organisational forms while maintaining principles – learning rather than rigidity.
- **Current pressures:** Cartel violence and climate-driven agricultural collapse reveal that strong internal governance doesn't automatically confer capacity to resist external threats.

**Relevance to Fabric:** The "mandar obedeciendo" principle offers a concrete operationalisation of accountability. Structural rotation and gender integration are institutionalised, not aspirational. The 2023 reorganisation demonstrates how to evolve structures while maintaining values – relevant for Fabric's emphasis on emergence.

### 4. Rojava Democratic Confederalism

**Context:** Feminist, ecological governance in North and East Syria since 2012, developed under conditions of civil war and existential threat. Based on Abdullah Öcalan's democratic confederalism philosophy.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Commune-Based Structure:**
Communes (~150 people) form the foundational unit, meeting monthly/fortnightly with all residents participating. Nested levels (commune → neighbourhood → district → canton) maintain local autonomy while enabling coordination. TEV-DEM operates as counter-power institution parallel to administration.

**Institutionalised Gender Parity:**
All positions require male-female co-leadership – structural requirement, not aspiration. Kongreya Star (women's confederation) operates autonomous structures while participating in mixed bodies. Both men and women study Jineology (women's science) as required education.

**Cooperative Economics:**
Production controlled via communes and people's councils. Cooperatives forbidden from becoming independent private businesses. Profit distributed: 30% future production, 50% workers, 20% cooperative services.

**Consensus Under Pressure:**
Decisions proceed when "good enough for now and safe enough to try." War context creates tension between deliberative process and emergency executive action – an ongoing challenge rather than resolved problem.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **What works under extreme pressure:** Gender parity produced real shifts in participation. Jineology's integration into education created analytical frameworks. Communes demonstrated deliberative democracy can function.
- **Implementation gaps:** Arab-majority regions required compromises with tribal leadership, bypassing democratic structures. Uneven consciousness development meant varied participation.
- **Ecological limits:** Social ecology principles guide policy, but practical sustainability limited by war and geopolitical vulnerability.

**Relevance to Fabric:** Demonstrates institutionalising principles (gender parity, cooperative economics) as structural requirements rather than values statements. The co-leadership model could inform Fabric's governance design. Shows both potential and limits of deliberative process under crisis conditions.

### 5. Participatory Budgeting

**Context:** Democratic innovation in collective resource allocation originating in Porto Alegre, Brazil (1989). Now >11,500 municipal processes globally, with UK implementations in Tower Hamlets, Scotland, and movement applications via platforms like Decidim.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Neighbourhood Assemblies:**
Residents articulate needs and propose projects, democratising information about resources while building deliberation capacity. Multiple rounds refine priorities.

**Needs-Weighted Allocation:**
Porto Alegre's model weighted distribution according to poverty indicators and infrastructure deficits – explicitly redistributive rather than per-capita. This addresses equity concerns central to Fabric's goals.

**Technical Committees and Feedback Loops:**
Implementation structures ensure decisions proceed from conception to completion. Regular reporting maintains participation across cycles.

**Digital Infrastructure (Decidim):**
Open-source platform supporting participatory budgeting across organisational forms – cooperatives, movements, networks. Modular architecture enables customisation.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **What makes PB effective vs tokenistic:** Budget adequacy (meaningful resources), institutional design (protecting decisions from override), investment in participation support (accompaniment, accessibility).
- **Demographic inequality:** Direct participation skews toward educated, wealthier residents with time. Paris invested in accessibility infrastructure to counter this.
- **Capture risks:** PB can perpetuate power distributions if marginalised communities lack information or skills. Limiting to small budget percentages represents deliberate containment.

**Relevance to Fabric:** Offers concrete mechanisms for collective resource allocation at network scale. Needs-weighted criteria could inform how resources flow between organisations of different capacities. The tension between meaningful participation and accessibility is directly relevant to Fabric's contribution model.

### 6. UK Restorative Justice

**Context:** Community-based accountability approaches operating across criminal justice, education, and community settings. Includes formal RJ (Restorative Justice Council standards) and transformative justice practices in activist communities.

#### Key Mechanisms

**Conferencing and Circles:**
Structured dialogue bringing together those harmed, those responsible, and supporters. Circle processes address multi-party situations. Facilitators focus dialogue without imposing solutions.

**Community Justice Panels:**
Volunteer mediators facilitate restorative meetings outside criminal justice (Sheffield, Somerset, Manchester). Costs substantially lower than court intervention (£160-£430 vs £1,240+).

**Transformative Justice Distinction:**
While RJ aims to restore relationships to pre-harm conditions, TJ questions whether original conditions were just. TJ addresses root causes (racism, patriarchy) through structural transformation, not just individual intervention. Originates from communities viewing state systems as harmful.

**Hybrid Approaches in Movements:**
UK activists often combine: RJ's dialogue and facilitation mechanisms with TJ's structural critique and non-reliance on state systems. Safer spaces policies, community accountability processes address harm without state involvement.

#### Lessons and Limitations

- **What works in UK:** Voluntary participation builds legitimacy. Embedded practice in organisational culture outperforms isolated interventions. Volunteer delivery builds local investment.
- **Gaps:** Victim participation remains low in formal schemes. Policy inconsistencies undermine implementation. Professionalisation risks alienating communities.
- **RJ vs TJ tension:** RJ's integration with formal systems may compromise liberatory potential. State institutions adopting RJ language while maintaining punitive structures represents co-option.

**Relevance to Fabric:** Conferencing and circle processes offer concrete tools for conflict work. The RJ/TJ distinction is crucial – Fabric's emphasis on structural change suggests TJ's framework may be more aligned. Volunteer-based community panels offer a model for the "Mediators" role without professionalisation.

## Cross-Source Synthesis

### Convergent Patterns

**Inverse Weighting / Protecting Small Voices:**
Both Mondragon (congressional representation) and participatory budgeting (needs-weighted allocation) demonstrate mechanisms to prevent larger entities dominating networks. Directly applicable to Fabric's challenge of balancing organisations of different scales.

**Institutionalised Rotation:**
Zapatistas (mandatory rotation), Enspiral (role cycling), and Rojava (term limits) all embed rotation structurally rather than leaving it to goodwill. This addresses the main report's concern about informal power accumulation.

**Material Autonomy:**
Zapatistas (land recovery), Mondragon (cooperative production), and Enspiral (collaborative funding) all emphasise building resource capacity independent of external funders. Confirms the main report's emphasis on network economy.

**Consent Over Consensus:**
Enspiral, Loomio, and Rojava all operationalise "safe enough to try" – distinct from both consensus and majority voting. Reinforces sociocratic principles in the main report.

**Gender as Structure:**
Rojava demonstrates institutionalising gender parity as requirement, not aspiration. Zapatistas embed women's participation in governance design. Offers concrete models beyond the main report's general recommendations.

### Divergent Approaches

**Scale Strategies:**
Mondragon scales through federation (maintaining local autonomy within larger structure). Zapatistas recently moved toward hyperlocal governance. Enspiral emphasises staying small. Different answers to the same problem.

**Relationship to State:**
Zapatistas and Rojava explicitly reject state engagement. Mondragon operates within legal frameworks. PB typically involves municipalities. UK RJ sits uneasily between community and formal systems. Fabric will need to navigate this spectrum.

**Economic Integration:**
Mondragon subsidiaries integrate with global capitalism (problematically). Zapatistas maintain relative isolation. Rojava cooperatives forbidden from becoming private businesses. Different trade-offs between resource capacity and principle preservation.

### Design Implications for Fabric

Based on this extended review, specific design considerations include:

**For Resource Distribution:**
- Consider Cobudget-style transparent allocation for shared resources
- Explore needs-weighted distribution criteria (not just per-capita)
- Build mechanisms like Mondragon's solidarity fund – mandatory not voluntary
- Weight representation inversely to organisation size

**For Collective Decision Making:**
- Implement advice process for operational decisions
- Use consent threshold ("safe enough to try") not consensus
- Consider Rojava's co-leadership model for key roles
- Build in mandatory rotation with term limits (not just guidelines)

**For Feedback & Learning:**
- Zapatistas' 2023 reorganisation demonstrates reviewing and restructuring while maintaining principles – build in periodic "constitutional" review
- Distinguish between operational feedback (regular) and structural evaluation (periodic)
- Document learning (Enspiral's handbook) to institutionalise lessons

**For Conflict Processing:**
- Develop staged escalation (Enspiral model) before crises
- Train volunteer mediators (community justice panel model) rather than professionalising
- Ground approach in transformative justice principles while using restorative techniques
- Address power differentials explicitly – "equal" processes can reinforce inequality

## Additional Observations

### Context Translation Requirements

Each of these examples emerged from specific conditions:

| Source | Key Contextual Factors | Translation Challenges for UK |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Enspiral | Small network, high-trust, tech-enabled | Scale, diverse constituencies, variable digital access |
| Mondragon | Basque identity, geographic concentration, 70-year development | Cultural rooting, legal frameworks, time horizon |
| Zapatistas | Indigenous knowledge, territorial base, external threat | Urban context, no land base, different threat environment |
| Rojava | Existential crisis, ideological commitment, military discipline | Peacetime organising, volunteer commitment, no unified enemy |
| Participatory budgeting | Municipal resources, institutional backing | Movement funding sources, legitimacy without state |
| UK RJ | Professional infrastructure, some state support | Grassroots capacity, avoiding co-option |

None can be transplanted directly. Fabric's design work involves identifying transferable principles and adapting mechanisms to UK social movement context.

### Research Gaps

Further investigation would benefit from:

- **Enspiral's failure modes:** What went wrong in enterprises that didn't survive?
- **Mondragon's non-federated subsidiaries:** What happens to workers without membership?
- **Zapatista 2023 reorganisation:** Detailed documentation of what prompted the change
- **Rojava's Arab-majority regions:** Why did democratic structures fail there?
- **UK PB:** Why Tower Hamlets didn't scale or sustain
- **TJ in UK movements:** Documented cases and lessons (limited public documentation)

## Conclusion

These extended sources confirm and elaborate the main report's findings. The convergence across very different contexts – Basque industrial cooperatives, Mexican Indigenous communities, Syrian revolutionary governance, Brazilian municipal innovation, UK justice reform – on similar mechanisms (consent over consensus, institutionalised rotation, material autonomy, structural equity measures) suggests these are not arbitrary choices but responses to common challenges in collective organisation.

For Fabric, the key insight is that principles require institutionalisation. Every successful example embeds values in structure, process, and requirement – not aspiration. The difference between "we believe in distributed power" and "all positions require rotation with term limits" is the difference between intention and mechanism.

The challenges are equally consistent: scale strains trust-based processes; external pressures create centralising tendencies; participation inequality reproduces power even in democratic structures; material constraints limit ideal implementation. These are not problems to solve but tensions to navigate – which is itself consistent with Fabric's commitment to living systems that adapt rather than fixed blueprints that break.
