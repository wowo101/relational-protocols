# Annotated Research Report: Fabric's Living Systems
## A Review of Existing Approaches

**Prepared for:** Fabric Incubation Team
**Date:** 5 February 2026

---

## Executive Summary

This report synthesises findings from 48 sources spanning movement organisation frameworks, political theory, complexity science, Indigenous knowledge systems, and transformative justice traditions. The research addresses how Fabric might structure its four living systems – Resource Distribution, Collective Decision Making, Feedback & Learning, and Conflict Processing & Transformation – to foster collective agency within UK social movements.

**Key findings:**

1. **Convergence on consent over consensus**: Multiple traditions (sociocracy, XR, Cynefin) agree that consent-based decision making ("Is it safe enough to try?") outperforms consensus ("Do we all agree?") at scale, enabling both speed and inclusion.

2. **The power paradox**: "Leaderless" or "horizontal" structures don't eliminate power – they make it invisible. Effective approaches explicitly map, distribute, and mitigate power rather than denying its existence.

3. **Conflict as resource**: The Cynefin tradition and transformative justice frameworks both reframe conflict not as failure but as energy and information – revealing system dynamics and creating opportunities for transformation.

4. **Feedback requires epistemic humility**: Indigenous critiques (Yunkaporta) and complexity approaches challenge Western assumptions about feedback, emphasising relational, contextual, and ecological ways of sensing that resist extraction and quantification.

5. **Resource flows must prefigure alternatives**: Approaches from commons theory (Benkler) to post-capitalist strategy (Heron et al.) agree that building autonomous resource circulation within movements is essential for both practical independence and embodying different relations.

6. **Strong alignment with Fabric's mission**: The reviewed approaches strongly support Fabric's emphasis on interconnectedness, emergence, and collective agency – while offering practical frameworks for operationalising these commitments.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Questions

This review addresses three specific questions from the Research Brief:

1. **What can we learn from existing approaches?** What do they propose, what have they learned, what are their potentials and limitations?

2. **Where do existing approaches agree and overlap, and where do they outline different visions and pathways?**

3. **What contexts do existing approaches respond to, and how similar to or different from our own context are they?**

The overarching question guiding this synthesis: *How can we structure these four living systems in order to support the flourishing of Fabric, with the ultimate aim of fostering collective agency?*

### 1.2 Living Systems Framework

Fabric draws on Navigate's Seven Living Systems as a starting framework, focusing on four systems for initial design:

- **Resource Distribution** – How resources flow within and between network layers
- **Collective Decision Making** – Who decides what, and how
- **Feedback & Learning** – How the network senses and adapts
- **Conflict Processing & Transformation** – How tensions become sources of growth

These four systems are understood as interdependent, each affecting the others. The goal is not to optimise each in isolation but to design for their dynamic interaction.

## 3. Thematic Synthesis by Living System

### 3.1 Resource Distribution

#### What approaches propose

**Commons-based peer production** (Benkler): The networked information economy enables large-scale cooperative production outside market relations. Unlike industrial-era organisation requiring capital concentration, distributed networks allow non-market production to achieve scale through shared motivation rather than financial incentives. Information goods can be freely shared without depletion, enabling "flourishing nonmarket sectors" – demonstrated by Wikipedia, Linux, and countless collaborative projects. The key insight for movements: production and distribution can be organised through "social norms, peer review, mutual pointing to valuable contributions, and reputation mechanisms" rather than market prices or managerial hierarchy.

**Public-Common Partnerships** (Heron, Milburn & Russell): Rather than waiting for post-revolutionary transformation, movements can pursue "contested reproduction" – using cracks in the current system to build alternative infrastructure now. Public-Common Partnerships redirect state resources toward commons-based initiatives (community land trusts, cooperative platforms, mutual aid networks). This requires strategic engagement with institutions while maintaining autonomy, building "institutions of dual power" that prefigure different relations while accumulating material capacity.

**Network economy** (Navigate, Fabric): Resources flow through relationships, not transactions. Navigate's Resource Flow system emphasises: transparent resourcing based on needs and capacity; mutual exchange between groups (skills, spaces, funding); and developing internal resource generation to reduce dependency on external funders. The aspiration is a "self-contained network economy" where value circulates within the movement.

**Symbiotic rather than extractive relations** (Fabric Mission): Resource sharing should embody the values the movement seeks to spread. This means attending to power differentials in resource access, ensuring flows don't reproduce dependency, and building systems where organisations support each other's flourishing.

#### Lessons learned

- **Dependency on external funding** is identified as a key vulnerability across sources. When funders set priorities, movements lose autonomy and strategic coherence.
- **Commons require boundaries and governance**: Benkler notes that successful commons are not simply "open" but have institutional frameworks determining access and use.
- **Scale changes dynamics**: What works for resource sharing between three organisations may not work for thirty. Systems need to adapt as networks grow.

#### Potentials and limitations

| Approach | Potentials | Limitations |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Commons-based production | Scales through participation; embodies alternative relations; reduces capital dependency | Works best for information goods; physical resources require different mechanisms; reputation systems can reproduce status hierarchies |
| Public-Common Partnerships | Accesses existing resources; builds dual power now; demonstrates alternatives | Risks co-optation; requires institutional navigation skills; dependent on political conditions |
| Internal network economy | Builds autonomy; strengthens relationships; prefigurative | Takes time to develop; may not meet all needs; can become insular |

#### Relevance to Fabric

Fabric's layered structure (Core, Network, External) maps well onto graduated resource-sharing models. The proposal for member contributions (7 hours/month or equivalent) represents an attempt to generate internal resources while maintaining accessibility. Key design questions include:

- How to ensure resource flows don't reproduce existing power differentials (larger organisations extracting from smaller ones)
- How to balance transparency with the practical need for some centralised coordination of shared resources
- How to make resource sharing feel generative rather than obligatory

### 3.2 Collective Decision Making

#### What approaches propose

**Consent vs Consensus** (Rau, XR, Snowden): Multiple sources converge on a crucial distinction. Consensus asks "Do we all agree?" – which becomes increasingly difficult at scale and can give veto power to individuals. Consent asks "Is it safe enough to try? Will it cause harm?" – a lower bar enabling faster decisions while maintaining collective accountability. Harm is defined narrowly: actions that prevent someone fulfilling their role or contradict shared purpose.

XR's formulation: "The key question shifts from 'Do we all like this?' to 'Is it safe enough to try? Will it cause harm?'"

Sociocracy (Rau): Decisions are made in circles with clear domains. The consent process includes: proposal → clarifying questions → reactions → amendments → consent round. Objections must be reasoned and based on harm to shared purpose, not personal preference. This enables both inclusion and efficiency by separating preferences from blocking concerns.

**Distributed leadership** (Nunes): Leadership is not binary (present/absent) but distributed. "Organising cores" – small groups with high commitment and coordination capacity – inevitably emerge even in "horizontal" movements. Rather than pretending otherwise, movements should make these visible, accountable, and plural. Leadership functions include: setting direction, maintaining coherence, building capacity, and representing the movement externally.

**Context-first decision-making** (Snowden): The Cynefin framework insists that context should precede authority. Different decision approaches suit different situations:
- **Simple domain**: Obvious cause-effect; best practice applies; authority can decide quickly
- **Complicated domain**: Expertise required; analysis before action; expert judgment
- **Complex domain**: Only understood through interaction; safe-to-fail experiments; distributed sensing
- **Chaotic domain**: No patterns; act to stabilise; strong leadership appropriate

Most movement decisions are in the Complex domain, where "the available evidence will support many different and contradictory hypotheses." This argues against both top-down direction and endless deliberation, toward iterative experimentation with rapid learning.

**Advice process** (XR, various): Anyone can make decisions by consulting those meaningfully affected and those with relevant expertise, then deciding independently while remaining accountable. This distributes decision-making while ensuring relevant input, avoiding both bottlenecks and uninformed action.

**Convergent Facilitation** (Kashtan): Rather than compromise or majority voting, facilitate convergence around needs. The process identifies what each party actually needs (vs positions), then seeks solutions that address all needs. When needs conflict, the question becomes whether creative solutions can integrate them.

#### Lessons learned

- **Informal power persists** (Freeman): "Structurelessness" doesn't eliminate power – it makes it invisible and unaccountable. Those with confidence, social capital, and time become de facto leaders while claiming horizontal process.
- **Facilitation is essential**: Decision-making processes require skilled facilitation to prevent domination by few voices and ensure genuine consent.
- **Role clarity enables autonomy**: When people know their mandate and accountability, they can act with confidence without seeking permission for every decision.
- **Scale requires differentiation**: Not every decision needs the same process. Distinguishing between operational, governance, and constitutional decisions enables appropriate depth.

#### Potentials and limitations

| Approach | Potentials | Limitations |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Consent-based | Fast and inclusive; enables experimentation; reduces veto power | Requires facilitation skill; can be gamed; needs clear harm definitions |
| Distributed leadership | Names reality; enables accountability; develops capacity | May concentrate power in "cores"; risks elite capture; requires trust |
| Advice process | Distributes authority; builds relationships; enables speed | Depends on good consultation; can be ignored; requires accountability |
| Convergent facilitation | Integrates needs; builds unity; creates commitment | Time-intensive; requires skilled facilitation; may not resolve all conflicts |

#### Relevance to Fabric

Fabric's proposed structure includes: Core circle for key decisions, sector-specific groupings, and movement-wide strategy discussions. The emphasis on "dedications" for core decision-makers represents an attempt to ensure alignment at the centre while enabling distributed action.

Key design questions include:
- How to make the relationship between Core and Network decisions clear and accountable
- How to handle situations where consent cannot be reached
- How to ensure advice processes genuinely consult affected parties rather than rubber-stamping decisions
- How to develop the facilitation capacity needed across the network

### 3.3 Feedback & Learning

#### What approaches propose

**Sensing and responding** (Navigate): Navigate's Feedback system emphasises continuous loops: noticing, sharing, integrating. This includes sensing internal dynamics (how are people feeling? what tensions exist?) and external environment (what's changing? what opportunities emerge?). Feedback should flow in all directions – not just upward reporting but genuine multi-directional sensing.

**Learning cycles** (Fabric Mission): Working in "plan-do-reflect" cycles that regularly review progress and decide on direction. This balances "explicitness (to facilitate alignment) and obliquity (to enable exploration)."

**Complexity-informed sensing** (Snowden): In complex domains, traditional feedback (measure → compare to target → adjust) doesn't work because the system responds non-linearly and retrospective analysis misses emergent patterns. Instead:
- Multiple parallel "sensors" gathering different kinds of information
- Attention to weak signals and anomalies
- Real-time distributed sense-making rather than periodic reviews
- Focus on patterns and trends rather than metrics

**Indigenous critiques of Western feedback** (Yunkaporta, Indigenous evaluation literature): Deep challenges to conventional feedback assumptions:

Yunkaporta's critique identifies "subtle fascism" in feedback paradigms that assume: linear causation, extractable knowledge, individual attribution, and universal metrics. These frameworks impose Western epistemology onto contexts where knowledge is relational, contextual, and ecological. "Feedback" becomes a mechanism for surveillance and control rather than genuine learning.

The Government of Canada's Indigenous evaluation framework proposes alternatives:
- Community-led rather than externally-imposed evaluation
- Holistic assessment that includes spiritual, emotional, physical, and mental dimensions
- Relational knowing that respects interconnectedness
- Time horizons spanning generations, not quarterly reports
- Process as important as outcomes

**Emergent evaluation** (Navigate, Indigenous sources): Rather than predefined metrics, allow evaluation criteria to emerge from the work itself. This requires trust in the process and willingness to be surprised by what becomes meaningful.

#### Lessons learned

- **Feedback systems can reproduce power relations**: Who defines what counts as success? Whose metrics matter? These are political questions.
- **Quantification has limits**: Not everything important can be measured. Overemphasis on metrics can crowd out what matters most.
- **Learning requires safety**: People share honestly when they trust information won't be used against them.
- **Reflection competes with action**: In crisis-mode movements, learning often "gets dropped." Building it into rhythm matters.

#### Potentials and limitations

| Approach | Potentials | Limitations |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Navigate's feedback loops | Integrates multiple directions; practical framework; connects to action | Can become formulaic; depends on culture of honesty |
| Plan-do-reflect cycles | Simple and memorable; normalises learning; builds in rhythm | Easy to skip "reflect"; may not capture complexity |
| Complexity-informed sensing | Matches actual system dynamics; attends to weak signals; distributed | Requires comfort with ambiguity; harder to communicate; resource-intensive |
| Indigenous/relational approaches | Decolonises knowledge; holistic; honours interconnectedness | May conflict with funder reporting requirements; requires cultural shift; slow |

#### Relevance to Fabric

Fabric's mission explicitly names "working in a learning cycle" as a core commitment. The challenge is to design feedback systems that:

- Honour relational and emergent knowing rather than imposing extractive metrics
- Flow between Core, Network, and External layers appropriately
- Create genuine psychological safety for honest sharing
- Resist pressure from funders to adopt conventional evaluation frameworks
- Build reflection into rhythm rather than treating it as optional

The Indigenous critique is particularly relevant given Fabric's commitment to "awareness of interconnectedness" – this applies to how knowledge is gathered and shared, not just content.

### 3.4 Conflict Processing & Transformation

#### What approaches propose

**Conflict as information and energy** (Snowden): Rather than viewing conflict as failure, the Cynefin approach treats it as revealing system dynamics. Snowden argues most organisational conflict occurs in the Complex domain where "the available evidence will support many different and contradictory hypotheses." Trying to resolve such conflict through compromise narrows available options and creates strategic blindness.

Alternative approach:
- **Map the conflict landscape**: What different positions exist? What assumptions underlie each?
- **Design safe-to-fail experiments**: Rather than seeking consensus, try multiple approaches simultaneously
- **Maintain coherent heterogeneity**: Differences within a framework provide adaptive capacity
- **Use ritual dissent**: Structured disagreement that refines proposals through challenge

**Transformative Justice** (Mingus): TJ emerged from communities – particularly disabled people of colour – for whom the criminal legal system was never protective. Core principles:
- Harm happens in context; addressing harm requires addressing conditions
- Accountability is relational, not punitive
- Community holds responsibility for prevention, intervention, and transformation
- Those harmed and those who cause harm both need support
- The goal is transformation of conditions, not just individual behaviour

TJ explicitly rejects both punitive approaches (punishment, exclusion) and restorative justice's tendency to focus on individual incidents without addressing systemic patterns.

**Community accountability** (Mingus, Hopeful Justice): Process elements include:
- Centring survivors' needs and agency
- Supporting accountability for those who caused harm (not abandoning them)
- Collective involvement in response
- Attention to conditions that enabled harm
- Long-term commitment to transformation

**Care and compassion in conflict** (Care ethics, Kashtan): Feminist care ethics insists that relationality be maintained even through conflict. This means:
- Treating all parties as worthy of care
- Attending to needs underlying positions
- Maintaining relationship as a value, not just instrumental goal
- Recognising care labour required in conflict processes

**Navigate's Conflict & Justice system**: Emphasises conflict as necessary for healthy groups, with stages: notice tension → name it → explore underlying needs → seek integration or accommodation → repair relationship. Distinguishes between interpersonal conflicts and systemic patterns requiring structural response.

#### Lessons learned

- **Avoidance creates bigger problems**: Unaddressed conflict doesn't disappear – it festers, erupts elsewhere, or leads to departure of valuable members.
- **Skill matters**: Conflict transformation requires specific competencies that most people don't have. Investment in capacity building is essential.
- **Power shapes conflict**: Conflicts between parties with unequal power require different approaches. "Equal" processes can reinforce inequality.
- **Time and resources required**: Genuine transformation takes sustained attention. Movements in crisis mode often can't provide this.
- **Prevention matters**: Building strong relationships and clear processes before conflict reduces severity when it arises.

#### Potentials and limitations

| Approach | Potentials | Limitations |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Cynefin (conflict as complexity) | Reframes conflict positively; enables experimentation; values difference | Can intellectualise emotional dynamics; may not address power; requires facilitation capacity |
| Transformative Justice | Addresses root causes; centrs survivors; builds community capacity | Resource-intensive; requires significant skill; may not suit all harms |
| Care ethics | Maintains relationships; humanises all parties; attends to needs | Can be used to pressure survivors to "forgive"; may prioritise relationship over justice |
| Navigate's approach | Practical stages; integrates with other systems; normalises conflict | May oversimplify complex situations; depends on goodwill |

#### Relevance to Fabric

Fabric's mission names "processing and resolution of existing conflicts between individuals, groups and organisations in a non-shaming way" as a key activity. The reviewed approaches suggest:

- Design for the full spectrum: interpersonal tensions through systemic patterns
- Build facilitation capacity across the network ("Mediators" role)
- Approach conflict as information about system dynamics, not just relationship failure
- Attend to power differentials in any process design
- Invest in prevention through relationship-building
- Resist pressure to "resolve" quickly when transformation requires time

## 4. Cross-Cutting Themes

### 4.1 Power: Distribution and Mitigation

Every tradition reviewed grapples with power – its inevitability, its dangers, and possibilities for transformation.

**Power-over / Power-with / Power-within**: This framework (from feminist and grassroots traditions) distinguishes:
- **Power-over**: Domination and control; zero-sum; associated with hierarchy
- **Power-with**: Collective capacity to act; generative; associated with solidarity
- **Power-within**: Personal agency and dignity; source of participation

Fabric's goal of "collective agency and power" is primarily about power-with, but this requires attending to how power-over dynamics infiltrate even intentionally horizontal spaces.

**Making power visible**: Jo Freeman's "Tyranny of Structurelessness" remains essential: claiming to be "leaderless" doesn't eliminate leadership – it makes it invisible and unaccountable. Those with time, confidence, social capital, and communication skills become de facto leaders while claiming equal process. The solution isn't abandoning structure but making it explicit, accountable, and plural.

**Mitigating privilege**: Sources address how movements can both leverage privilege (resources, access, visibility that privileged members can bring) while mitigating its reproduction (ensuring those with privilege don't dominate, that diverse perspectives shape direction). Strategies include:

- Explicit role rotation with term limits
- Decision-making quotas or protocols ensuring diverse voices
- Training in recognising and interrupting privilege dynamics
- Accountability structures that privilege can't circumvent
- Resource redistribution toward marginalised groups

### 4.2 Autonomy-Alignment Polarity

Every source navigates the tension between group coherence and individual/subgroup freedom.

**Both essential**: Nunes argues movements need both "platforms" (shared infrastructure, common ground) and "events" (distributed action, local adaptation). Too much alignment stifles innovation and local responsiveness; too little leads to fragmentation and strategic incoherence.

**Nested sovereignty**: Multiple sources propose fractal structures where groups have genuine autonomy within their domain while connecting to larger purposes. Navigate's circles, XR's SOS, and sociocratic models all share this pattern.

**Clear boundaries enable freedom**: When people know their mandate – what they can decide independently – they act with more confidence. Unclear boundaries create either paralysis (constant checking) or conflict (stepping on toes).

**Emergent alignment**: Fabric's emphasis on emergence suggests alignment shouldn't be front-loaded through detailed plans but cultivated through relationships, shared practice, and iterative learning. Strategy "is not developed by movement leaders and then adopted by others – it's the priorities and orientations the movement as a whole is actually adopting as an outcome of all the interactions."

### 4.3 Emergence vs Structure

Sources differ on how much structure to design upfront versus allowing to emerge.

**Constraint-driven organising** (Fabric): Structure emerges in response to constraints, not as predetermined blueprint. "The shape an organisation or movement takes is determined by the moment they are responding to."

**Good-enough structures** (Navigate, XR): Don't over-design. Create minimal structures that are "safe enough to try," then adapt based on experience. This requires comfort with messiness and ongoing learning.

**Structure enables emergence** (Cynefin): This isn't structure vs emergence but structure for emergence. Safe-to-fail experiments, clear decision rights, and feedback loops all require structural support to function.

**Too little structure reproduces power** (Freeman): Without explicit structure, informal structures dominate – typically reinforcing existing privileges. Some structure is essential for equity.

### 4.4 Scale and Fractal Organisation

As networks grow, coordination challenges multiply. Sources offer various responses:

**Self-similar structures**: Sociocracy and XR use nested circles operating by similar principles at different scales. This enables local autonomy while maintaining coherent process.

**Differentiated processes for different decisions**: Not every decision needs the same treatment. Quick operational choices use advice process; strategic direction uses broader consent; constitutional changes require extended deliberation.

**Organising cores**: Nunes argues some centralisation is inevitable and healthy – the question is whether cores are visible, accountable, plural, and rotational.

**Technology for coordination**: Benkler emphasises how networked communication enables coordination at scales previously requiring hierarchy. But technology amplifies existing dynamics – it doesn't solve political challenges.

### 4.5 Relationality and Interconnectedness

Fabric's central commitment to "interconnectedness" finds support across traditions:

**Indigenous knowledge systems**: Knowing is relational; individuals exist within webs of relationship extending to land, ancestors, future generations, and other-than-human beings. This isn't metaphor but epistemology.

**Feminist care ethics**: Humans are fundamentally relational; autonomy is achieved through relationship, not despite it. Care is not merely instrumental but constitutive of social life.

**Complexity theory**: Systems are irreducibly interconnected; intervening anywhere affects everywhere; separation is analytical convenience, not reality.

**Commons theory**: Production itself is relational; value emerges from cooperation and shared resources; extractive models that treat relationships as mere means destroy their own foundations.

This convergence suggests Fabric's emphasis on interconnectedness isn't peripheral but foundational – affecting not just values but how every system should be designed.

## 5. Answers to Research Questions

### 5.1 What can we learn from existing approaches?

**What they propose:**

Across traditions, existing approaches converge on several key proposals:

1. **Consent over consensus** for scalable, inclusive decision-making that enables action while maintaining accountability

2. **Distributed authority** through clear mandates, advice processes, and nested circles that avoid both bottlenecks and chaos

3. **Explicit attention to power** – making leadership visible and accountable rather than pretending it doesn't exist

4. **Conflict as resource** – treating tensions as information about system dynamics and opportunities for transformation

5. **Resource flows that prefigure alternatives** – building internal economies that embody different relations

6. **Learning built into rhythm** – regular reflection cycles that become habit rather than optional extra

7. **Relational approaches** to feedback, justice, and coordination that resist extractive, quantified, or punitive logics

**What they have learned:**

Key lessons across sources include:

- "Structurelessness" creates invisible hierarchies; explicit structure enables accountability
- Scale requires differentiated processes – not everything needs full deliberation
- Facilitation capacity is essential and often underinvested
- Unaddressed conflict doesn't disappear – it metastasises
- Learning competes with crisis response; protecting reflection time matters
- Power differentials affect every process; "equal" procedures can reinforce inequality
- Resource dependency on external funders compromises strategic autonomy
- Transformation takes longer than crisis-mode movements often allow

**Potentials:**

- Consent-based approaches can be both faster and more inclusive than consensus
- Distributed leadership develops capacity across the movement
- Commons-based resource sharing strengthens relationships while building material capacity
- Transformative justice addresses root causes rather than just symptoms
- Complexity-informed approaches match actual system dynamics

**Limitations:**

- Most approaches require significant facilitation skill that movements often lack
- Time and resources for deep process work compete with urgent action
- Approaches developed in specific contexts may not translate directly
- Tension between relational/emergent approaches and funder expectations
- Scale changes everything – what works for 30 people may not work for 300

### 5.2 Where do approaches agree, overlap, and diverge?

**Areas of agreement:**

1. **Power must be addressed explicitly**: Freeman, Nunes, XR, Indigenous approaches, feminist care ethics – all insist power dynamics must be named and structured, not ignored

2. **Consent outperforms consensus at scale**: Sociocracy, XR, Cynefin all converge on "safe enough to try" as better question than "does everyone agree"

3. **Relationships are foundational**: Indigenous epistemology, care ethics, Navigate, Fabric mission – all treat relationality as constitutive, not merely instrumental

4. **Emergence requires cultivation**: You can't design emergence top-down, but you can create conditions for it – shared practice, trust, feedback loops, protected experimentation

5. **Resource autonomy matters**: Commons theory, post-capitalist strategy, movement frameworks all identify external funding dependency as vulnerability

**Areas of productive tension:**

1. **How much structure upfront?** Navigate and XR provide relatively detailed frameworks; complexity approaches emphasise letting structure emerge; both agree on iteration

2. **Individual vs collective focus**: Transformative justice centres survivors' needs; complexity approaches prioritise system dynamics; care ethics seeks to hold both

3. **Speed vs depth**: Advice process enables quick decisions; convergent facilitation takes time; Cynefin suggests matching process to domain

4. **Role of leadership**: Nunes argues for making organising cores visible; others emphasise horizontal distribution; all agree on accountability

**Divergent visions:**

1. **Relationship to existing institutions**: Public-Common Partnerships engage state resources strategically; some Indigenous approaches reject integration with colonial systems; Plan C's "Block, Modify, Transform" sees these as complementary

2. **Metrics and evaluation**: Mainstream approaches measure outcomes quantitatively; Indigenous critiques challenge whether this serves communities; complexity approaches focus on patterns over metrics

3. **Conflict resolution vs transformation**: Some approaches aim to "resolve" conflict (reach agreement, move on); TJ and Cynefin argue for ongoing engagement with underlying conditions

### 5.3 What contexts do approaches respond to?

**Movement organisation frameworks (XR, Navigate):**
- Context: Large-scale social movements needing coordination across dispersed groups
- Scale: Thousands of participants, multiple regions/nations
- Conditions: Climate crisis urgency, volunteer capacity limits, activist burnout
- **Similarity to Fabric**: High – UK movements, network-of-networks, polycrisis context

**Political organisation theory (Nunes, Rau, Benkler):**
- Context: Academic analysis of movements and organisations
- Scale: Theoretical frameworks applicable at various scales
- Conditions: Post-2008 movements (Occupy, 15M, etc.), platform capitalism
- **Similarity to Fabric**: Medium-high – useful frameworks but require translation

**Post-capitalist transition (Heron et al.):**
- Context: UK/European left strategy post-Corbyn
- Scale: National/international political economy
- Conditions: Climate crisis, austerity, rising authoritarianism
- **Similarity to Fabric**: High – same political-economic context, similar analysis

**Complexity/Cynefin (Snowden):**
- Context: Originally management consulting, increasingly applied to social systems
- Scale: Primarily organisational (10s-100s); principles scale to larger systems
- Conditions: VUCA (volatile, uncertain, complex, ambiguous) environments
- **Similarity to Fabric**: Medium – relevant frameworks but often corporate language

**Indigenous approaches:**
- Context: Majority World and Indigenous communities resisting colonial extraction
- Scale: From local communities to international Indigenous movements
- Conditions: Ongoing colonisation, extraction, cultural genocide
- **Similarity to Fabric**: Medium-variable – crucial perspectives but requires careful translation; avoid appropriation

**Transformative Justice:**
- Context: Communities where criminal legal system fails (especially BIPOC, disabled, queer communities)
- Scale: Community-level, primarily US origins
- Conditions: Carceral state, movement harm, accountability needs
- **Similarity to Fabric**: Medium-high – relevant to UK movements though different legal/cultural context

**Care ethics:**
- Context: Feminist philosophy and practice
- Scale: Theoretical framework applicable at multiple scales
- Conditions: Critique of atomistic individualism, marketisation of care
- **Similarity to Fabric**: High – directly relevant to relational/interconnectedness focus

**Overall assessment**: Most reviewed approaches emerge from contexts with significant similarities to Fabric's situation. The UK social movement context, polycrisis conditions, network-of-networks structure, and commitment to prefigurative practice all have strong precedents in the literature. The main translations required are:

- From US to UK context (especially TJ, Indigenous approaches)
- From corporate/consulting to movement context (Cynefin)
- From theoretical to practical application (political organisation theory)
- From single-organisation to network-of-networks scale (most frameworks)

## 6. Mission Alignment Assessment

### 6.1 Points of Natural Alignment

**Interconnectedness as foundation**: Fabric's central commitment finds strong support across traditions – Indigenous epistemology, care ethics, complexity theory, and commons approaches all treat relationality as constitutive. This isn't just values alignment but convergence on how systems actually work.

**Collective agency through emergence**: Fabric's understanding that "collective agency is an emergent phenomenon" aligns with complexity approaches, Nunes' analysis of movements, and Indigenous knowledge systems. The implication – creating conditions rather than determining outcomes – is methodologically consistent across sources.

**Network-based coordination**: Benkler's analysis of networked peer production, Navigate's living systems, and XR's distributed structure all support Fabric's vision of "networks as producers of value" with "knowledge residing in relationships."

**Prefigurative practice**: The commitment to "practising now what we want to see in the future" aligns with transformative justice's emphasis on embodying different relations, care ethics' attention to process, and commons approaches to resource sharing.

**Layered structure**: Fabric's Core/Network/External layers map well onto sociocratic nested circles, XR's distributed structure, and Nunes' analysis of core-periphery dynamics.

### 6.2 Potential Tensions

**Learning cycles vs emergence**: Fabric commits to "plan-do-reflect" cycles with "explicitness to facilitate alignment." Indigenous critiques and some complexity approaches caution against over-systematising learning, which can become extractive. The tension is between structured reflection and emergent knowing – both matter, and the balance requires ongoing attention.

**Membership requirements vs accessibility**: The proposed contributions (7 hours/month or equivalent) may create barriers for those with care responsibilities, precarious work, or health limitations. Care ethics would emphasise designing for actual people's situations, not ideal participants.

**Role of "Elders circle for discernment"**: While this could support wise decision-making, the term "elders" requires careful attention to whose wisdom is centred. Indigenous approaches might question whether this term is being appropriated, and whether it risks concentrating power in ways that contradict distributed leadership principles.

**Resource dependency**: Building a self-contained network economy takes time. In the interim, Fabric may need external funding, which sources consistently identify as creating compromise. Managing this transition requires strategic attention.

### 6.3 Questions for Design

Based on this alignment assessment, key design questions for Fabric include:

**Resource Distribution:**
- How can contribution requirements be designed for accessibility without creating free-rider dynamics?
- What mechanisms ensure larger organisations don't extract disproportionate value?
- How to balance network resource generation with interim external funding needs?

**Collective Decision Making:**
- How does the "Elders circle" relate to broader network consent?
- What constitutes "harm" that can block consent – and who defines it?
- How to ensure the Core remains accountable to Network and External layers?

**Feedback & Learning:**
- How to honour relational/emergent knowing while maintaining enough structure for accountability?
- How to resist funder pressure for conventional evaluation frameworks?
- How to build genuine psychological safety for honest sharing?

**Conflict Processing:**
- What capacity building is needed for the "Mediators" role?
- How to handle conflicts involving power differentials (e.g., between organisations with very different resources)?
- What's the relationship between interpersonal conflict resolution and addressing systemic patterns?

### 6.4 Recommendations for Living Systems Design

Based on this review, the following principles are recommended for Fabric's living systems design:

1. **Consent-based decision making** as default, with clear definition of "harm" that can constitute a reasoned objection

2. **Explicit power mapping** at network and organisational levels, with regular review and redistribution mechanisms

3. **Graduated participation** enabling different levels of engagement without creating second-class membership

4. **Relational feedback approaches** that resist extractive quantification while maintaining accountability

5. **Conflict capacity building** as ongoing investment, not just crisis response

6. **Resource flows designed for mutuality** – explicitly attending to power differentials in sharing arrangements

7. **Protected learning rhythms** built into network cycle rather than added optionally

8. **Translation rather than transplantation** of approaches from different contexts

## 7. Pointers for Further Work

### 7.1 Areas Requiring Deeper Exploration

**Specific mechanism design**: This review identifies principles but not detailed procedures. Further work should develop specific protocols for:
- Consent decision-making at network scale
- Resource sharing agreements between organisations
- Conflict escalation and intervention processes
- Learning cycle structures and rhythms

**Power mapping methodology**: How to actually map power dynamics in Fabric's network – who has it, how it flows, where it concentrates – in ways that inform intervention.

**Indigenous consultation**: This review draws on published Indigenous scholarship, but any adoption of Indigenous frameworks should involve direct relationship with Indigenous knowledge holders. This is especially important given critiques of extraction and appropriation.

**Care labour distribution**: Who does the relational work of network maintenance? How to ensure this labour is visible, valued, and shared rather than falling on those already marginalised?

**Technology choices**: What platforms and tools support the kind of distributed, relational coordination Fabric envisions? Current options (Mobilize, HumHub, Hylo) each have trade-offs worth examining.

### 7.2 Sources to Explore Further

Several areas warrant additional research:

- **Enspiral and Loomio**: Network coordination with explicit resource and decision-making protocols
- **Mondragon cooperatives**: Large-scale democratic economic organisation
- **Zapatista governance**: Indigenous-rooted autonomous organising at scale
- **Rojava democratic confederalism**: Feminist, ecological network governance under crisis conditions
- **Participatory budgeting**: Specific mechanisms for collective resource allocation
- **Restorative justice developments in UK context**: Local application of accountability frameworks

### 7.3 Pilot and Iteration Approach

Given the emphasis on emergence across sources, Fabric's living systems should be developed through:

1. **Start minimal**: Begin with simplest viable processes, adding complexity only as needed
2. **Multiple pilots**: Try different approaches in different contexts simultaneously
3. **Regular review cycles**: Built-in reflection on what's working
4. **Documented learning**: Capture lessons for the network and movement more broadly
5. **Comfortable incompleteness**: Resist pressure to have everything figured out before starting

### Appendix C: Glossary of Key Terms

**Collective agency**: Capacity to act together toward shared purposes; emergent from interactions of individuals, groups, and organisations; more than sum of individual capacities

**Commons**: Institutional framework enabling action through open access without requiring payment; contrasts with property-based markets

**Consent (decision-making)**: Agreement that proposal is "safe enough to try" and won't cause harm; contrasts with consensus requiring full agreement

**Convergent facilitation**: Process seeking solutions that address all parties' needs rather than compromise or majority

**Cynefin**: Sense-making framework distinguishing Simple, Complicated, Complex, and Chaotic domains; guides appropriate decision-making approaches

**Organising cores**: Small groups with high commitment and coordination capacity that inevitably emerge in movements; making them visible enables accountability

**Power-over/power-with/power-within**: Framework distinguishing domination, collective capacity, and personal agency

**Safe-to-fail experiments**: Interventions designed for rapid learning in complex systems; includes early warning signs and response strategies

**Transformative Justice**: Community-based approach to harm that addresses root causes rather than punishment; centres survivors while supporting accountability
