# The Zürcher Ressourcen Modell and Fabric's Decision-Making Systems

## An Exploration of Somatic Self-Management for Collective Agency

---

## 1. What is the Zürcher Ressourcen Modell?

The Zürcher Ressourcen Modell (ZRM – Zurich Resource Model) is a self-management training developed in the 1990s by Maja Storch and Frank Krause at the University of Zurich. Originally designed to protect trainee teachers from burnout, it has since been applied across clinical, educational, and organisational settings. Its distinctive contribution is the systematic integration of *cognitive*, *emotional*, and *physiological* dimensions into a single goal-pursuit process – bridging the gap between knowing what you want to do and actually doing it.

### 1.1 Theoretical Foundations

The ZRM draws on three principal intellectual streams:

**Motivational psychology – the Rubikon Process.** The ZRM adapts Heckhausen and Gollwitzer's Rubikon Model of action phases, expanding it from four to five stages: Needs → Motive → Intention → Pre-actional Preparation → Action. The metaphor is Caesar at the Rubicon – the crucial, irreversible crossing from "I'd like to" into "I will." The ZRM's core insight is that this crossing requires more than rational commitment; it requires the alignment of conscious intention with unconscious, body-based motivation.

**Neuroscience – Damasio's somatic markers.** Antonio Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis (developed from 1991, formally published 1996) proposes that emotional processes, registered as bodily sensations, guide decision-making – particularly in complex, uncertain situations where rational calculation alone is insufficient. A "somatic marker" is a body signal (butterflies, tension, warmth, constriction) that functions as an automatic evaluative response, drawing on accumulated experience to bias choices before conscious deliberation has concluded. The ZRM makes these markers the *primary navigational instrument* for identifying what someone genuinely wants – not what they think they should want.

**Two-system theory.** Drawing on the dual-process tradition (popularised by Kahneman, but with distinct ZRM inflections via LeDoux and Roth), the ZRM distinguishes between "the mind" (*Verstand*) – conscious, analytical, verbal – and "the unconscious" – fast, holistic, body-based, image-rich. The method's power lies in its insistence that effective self-management requires *synchronisation* of both systems, not the dominance of one over the other.

### 1.2 The Five-Phase Process

1. **Needs phase**: Participants view a curated set of images and attend to their somatic responses – which images produce positive body signals? This bypasses verbal rationalisation and accesses what the body already knows about what matters.

2. **Motive phase**: Using the *Ideenkorb* (idea basket) – a structured group process – participants explore the associations, feelings, and meanings their chosen image evokes. This is explicitly constructivist: meaning is not extracted by an expert but generated by the person in dialogue with peers.

3. **Intention phase**: Participants develop a *Motto-Ziel* (motto goal) – not a specific, measurable target but a general attitude goal expressed in figurative, embodied language ("I am a tree with deep roots and wide branches"). Research suggests such attitude goals are experienced as more authentically one's own than SMART goals, and they synchronise conscious and unconscious motivation.

4. **Pre-actional preparation**: Resources are gathered and activated through *embodiment* (physical postures, gestures, movements that anchor the motto goal in the body), *priming* (environmental cues placed in daily life to reactivate the goal state), and *if-then plans* (Gollwitzer's implementation intentions, adapted to secure the motto goal against predictable obstacles).

5. **Action phase**: When needs, goals, and resources are aligned across cognitive, emotional, and somatic registers, action follows with less friction and more sustainability.

### 1.3 Key Methods

- **Somatic marker tracking**: Participants learn to notice and interpret body signals as evaluative information – developing what might be called *somatic literacy*.
- **Affect balance** (*Affektbilanz*): A technique for checking whether a goal formulation produces a strongly positive somatic marker – the criterion for a good motto goal.
- **Embodiment**: Physical practices that anchor intentions in the body, based on research showing bidirectional links between posture/movement and psychological states.
- **Priming**: Strategic placement of sensory cues (objects, images, scents) in one's environment to maintain activation of the desired neural network.
- **If-then plans**: Pre-committed responses to anticipated situations, creating "strategic automaticity" – the behaviour is triggered by the situation rather than requiring effortful self-control in the moment.

## 2. Empirical Validity

### 2.1 The Evidence Base for ZRM Specifically

The ZRM has a modest but growing body of empirical support:

- **Cortisol and stress reactivity** (Storch, Gaab, Küttel, Stüssi & Fend, 2007 – *Health Psychology*): The strongest study. An RCT showed that ZRM-trained healthy subjects had significantly lower cortisol responses to a standardised psychosocial stressor (Trier Social Stress Test), with effects persisting at 4-month follow-up. This is notable because it demonstrates psychoneuroendocrine impact – the training changed not just self-report but hormonal stress responses.

- **Eating disorders feasibility trial** (Storch et al., 2011 – *American Journal of Psychotherapy*): A small RCT (n=19) found that ZRM-based affect regulation training, added to standard inpatient treatment, produced significant improvements in down-regulating negative affect at 3 and 12 months. Explicitly a feasibility study with acknowledged sample size limitations.

- **Asthma self-efficacy**: An RCT found improved self-efficacy when ZRM was added to standard asthma education, though long-term outcome translation remained uncertain.

- **Social resources in students**: A study with 247 German university students (intervention vs. waiting control) evaluated ZRM's effects on perception and use of social resources.

- **Pregnancy and stress**: ZRM tested for activating personal resources during pregnancy to reduce stress and increase well-being.

**Assessment**: The evidence is *preliminary but encouraging*. The cortisol study provides genuine psychophysiological evidence, and the ongoing research programme at the University of Zurich suggests sustained academic engagement. However, large-scale, multi-site RCTs with active control conditions are lacking. The ZRM is better understood as a theoretically sophisticated, clinically tested method with plausible mechanisms than as an approach with robust, replicated outcome evidence.

### 2.2 The Evidence Base for ZRM's Constituent Parts

The picture improves considerably when we assess the component theories:

**Somatic marker hypothesis**: Damasio's framework has generated substantial research. The Iowa Gambling Task paradigm showed that patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage – who lack somatic markers – make significantly poorer decisions. However, a major critical review (Dunn, Dalgleish & Lawrence, 2006) raised concerns about the specificity of the IGT evidence, the parsimony of the somatic feedback mechanism, and alternative explanations. More recent work (Overskeid, 2021 – *Frontiers in Psychology*) argues the hypothesis may actually explain *more* than Damasio claims – that emotions necessarily decide all voluntary action. The core insight – that bodily-emotional signals play a functional role in decision-making, especially under complexity and uncertainty – is now broadly accepted in affective neuroscience, even if the specific mechanism remains debated.

**Implementation intentions** (Gollwitzer): This component has perhaps the strongest evidence base of any element in the ZRM. A large meta-analytic literature (d = 0.65 in Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006) confirms that if-then plans substantially improve goal attainment across diverse domains. The mechanism – creating "strategic automaticity" by delegating action control to environmental cues – is well-supported by neuroimaging studies showing reduced need for effortful control. Critically, a related meta-analysis showed a significant intention-behaviour gap (strong intentions alone: d = 0.66 for commitment but only d = 0.33 for behaviour), providing the *problem* that implementation intentions solve.

**Embodiment**: The bidirectional relationship between bodily states and psychological experience is well-supported (though the "power posing" literature has been contentious). The broader embodied cognition research programme (Lakoff & Johnson, Varela, Barsalou) provides strong theoretical grounding for the ZRM's use of physical practices to anchor psychological states.

**Dual-process theory**: The distinction between fast/automatic and slow/deliberative processing is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive science, though the precise nature and boundaries of the "two systems" remain debated.

**Assessment of the whole**: The ZRM's theoretical architecture is built on well-evidenced components, even where the specific ZRM integration of those components awaits more rigorous testing. The approach is more empirically grounded than many self-development methods, and its explicit engagement with neuroscience and motivational psychology gives it more theoretical depth than typical coaching frameworks.

## 3. Connections to McGilchrist and Related Approaches

### 3.1 McGilchrist's Hemispheric Thesis

Iain McGilchrist's *The Master and His Emissary* (2009) and *The Matter with Things* (2021) argue that the two brain hemispheres attend to the world in fundamentally different ways:

- The **right hemisphere** offers broad, contextual, embodied attention – it sees wholes, relationships, the living particular. It is deeply connected to the body, to empathy, to metaphor, to the sense of things as interconnected and in flux.
- The **left hemisphere** offers focused, analytical, abstracting attention – it categorises, measures, manipulates. It is the realm of explicit language, fixed categories, instrumental control.

McGilchrist's central claim is that Western civilisation has progressively come to operate in a "left hemisphere mode" – prioritising abstraction, control, measurement, and fragmentation over the relational, embodied, contextual knowing of the right hemisphere. This isn't about location in the brain so much as about two *modes of attending* to the world.

**Empirical status**: McGilchrist's work is genuinely scholarly (referencing ~5,500 papers in *The Matter with Things*) but has attracted serious neuroscientific criticism. A metaanalytic assessment by Spezio (2019, *Religion, Brain & Behavior*) found that recent meta-analyses of hemispheric lateralisation do not support the strong, oppositional claims McGilchrist makes – particularly regarding empathy and non-literal language being exclusively right-lateralised. The philosophical-to-neuroscientific bridge remains underspecified. However, the *functional* distinction between modes of attention – narrow/analytical vs. broad/contextual/embodied – is more defensible than the strong hemispheric localisation claim.

### 3.2 The ZRM–McGilchrist Resonance

The conceptual alignment between ZRM and McGilchrist is striking, even though they emerge from different intellectual traditions:

| Dimension | ZRM | McGilchrist |
|-----------|-----|-------------|
| **Core problem** | Rational intention alone doesn't produce action; the body and unconscious must be aligned | Left-hemisphere dominance produces a world of abstraction disconnected from lived reality |
| **Role of the body** | Somatic markers as primary navigational instruments for genuine motivation | Right hemisphere deeply connected to embodied experience; "only the right hemisphere has a whole body image" |
| **Knowledge type valued** | Implicit, image-based, feeling-toned knowing alongside explicit reasoning | Right hemisphere knowledge "resists general terms, has to be experienced, depends on encounter" |
| **Language** | Motto goals use metaphorical, figurative language to bridge conscious and unconscious | Metaphor is "largely a right hemisphere capacity" and "at the root of all meaning" |
| **Images** | Image-based process for accessing unconscious needs and motivations | Right hemisphere processes through images, gestalts, wholes |
| **Integration** | Both systems ("mind" and "unconscious") must be synchronised; neither alone suffices | Both hemispheres are needed; the right should be "master" with left as "emissary" |
| **Interconnectedness** | Resources understood as neural networks; identity as relational pattern | Reality is fundamentally interconnected; separation is left-hemisphere artefact |

This convergence suggests that the ZRM could be understood as a *practical methodology for cultivating right-hemisphere engagement in decision-making* – providing structured techniques for what McGilchrist describes philosophically.

### 3.3 Gendlin's Focusing

Eugene Gendlin's Focusing method (1978) is an important bridge. Focusing teaches systematic attention to the "felt sense" – a pre-verbal, body-based sense of a situation's meaning. Like the ZRM's somatic marker work, it involves:

- Directing non-judging attention to bodily experience
- Waiting for implicit meaning to emerge, rather than imposing conceptual frameworks
- Using images and metaphors (not analytical language) to articulate what the body knows

Gendlin explicitly developed collective applications through Changes International in the 1970s, exploring how focusing-style listening could support group decision-making and organisational processes. His philosophical work (*A Process Model*) provides deep theoretical grounding for the claim that bodies can "feel complex situations" and "come up with answers to complex human living questions we cannot figure out."

### 3.4 Other Related Approaches

- **Convergent Facilitation** (Miki Kashtan): Identifies underlying needs (not positions) and seeks integration – resonates with ZRM's emphasis on accessing genuine needs beneath rational preferences.
- **Presencing / Theory U** (Scharmer): Uses somatic and contemplative practices to access "deeper sources of knowing" in organisational change processes.
- **Cynefin safe-to-fail experiments** (Snowden): For complex situations where rational analysis cannot determine the best course – resonates with the ZRM's acceptance that the unconscious/body may perceive patterns that the analytical mind cannot.

## 4. Application to Fabric's Decision-Making Systems

### 4.1 The Three Design Criteria

You specified three criteria for decision-making systems. Here is how the ZRM addresses each:

#### a) Enabling agency (actually doing the agreed things)

This is the ZRM's *central problem*. The entire Rubikon Process is designed to solve the intention-behaviour gap – the well-documented phenomenon (Gollwitzer's meta-analysis: d = 0.66 for commitment, d = 0.33 for behaviour) that even strong intentions frequently fail to produce action.

The ZRM's solution is multi-layered:

1. **Ensure the intention is genuinely wanted** (not just rationally endorsed) by using somatic markers to check alignment between conscious goals and unconscious motivation. A decision that produces a strongly positive somatic marker is more likely to be enacted because the whole organism – not just the verbal, analytical mind – is behind it.

2. **Bridge intention and action** through embodiment and priming – creating bodily and environmental supports that keep the intention active in daily life rather than leaving it as an abstract commitment.

3. **Create strategic automaticity** through if-then plans that pre-commit specific behavioural responses to anticipated situations, reducing the self-control burden at the moment of action.

**Implication for Fabric**: The design scaffolding's emphasis on consent-based decision-making ("safe enough to try") already moves in the right direction – it lowers the bar from "full agreement" to "no blocking objections." But ZRM thinking suggests going further: before or during the consent process, participants could be invited to check their *somatic response* to a proposal. A decision that passes consent rationally but produces widespread negative somatic markers (tension, constriction, unease) may not be enacted with full energy. Conversely, a decision that evokes positive body signals is more likely to generate genuine follow-through.

This doesn't mean making somatic responses into veto power (that would reproduce the consensus problem at a different level). Rather, it means *treating body-level response as information to be surfaced and integrated into the decision process* – adding a sensing dimension to the rational and relational dimensions already present.

#### b) Incorporating "right hemisphere"/somatic information

The ZRM provides concrete, learnable techniques for exactly this:

- **Image work**: Using curated images to access pre-verbal, body-based knowing about what matters. This could be adapted for collective use – e.g., at the start of a strategy process, inviting participants to select images that resonate somatically with their sense of the situation, then exploring the collective pattern.

- **Somatic literacy training**: Teaching people to notice and interpret body signals as information. In a movement context, this could be embedded in facilitation training – facilitators who can read the room's *somatic field* (collective tension, energy, constriction, flow) have access to information that verbal communication alone doesn't provide.

- **Motto goals using metaphorical language**: Instead of (or alongside) SMART strategic goals, developing network-level "motto goals" – figurative expressions of shared orientation that speak to the whole person, not just the analytical mind. Fabric's existing use of metaphor ("connective tissue," "living systems," the fabric metaphor itself) already moves in this direction.

- **Embodiment practices**: Physical exercises, movement, and posture work built into meeting rhythms – not as optional extras but as integral parts of the decision-making process. This aligns with Fabric's mission emphasis on creating "embodied experiences of interconnectedness."

**Connection to the design scaffolding**: Principle 3.2 ("Honour relational knowing; resist extractive metrics") and the Indigenous critiques of Western feedback paradigms (Yunkaporta) both point toward exactly this – ways of knowing that are embodied, relational, and resistant to quantification. The ZRM offers *practical techniques* for operationalising what these sources describe philosophically.

#### c) Fostering a sense of interconnectedness

This is where the application becomes most interesting and most speculative. The ZRM was developed primarily for individual self-management. Extending it to collective contexts requires creative adaptation, but the theoretical foundations support it:

**The body as site of connection.** McGilchrist argues that the right hemisphere – which is more deeply connected to embodied experience – is also the hemisphere of empathy, of sensing others, of understanding context and relationship. If somatic awareness opens us to our own body's knowing, it also opens us to resonance with others' embodied states. Sharing somatic responses in a group ("When I sit with this proposal, I notice warmth in my chest and a settling in my belly") creates a different quality of connection than sharing opinions ("I think this is a good idea").

**Collective somatic sensing.** Several traditions (Gendlin's focusing-in-groups, presencing practices, somatics-based facilitation) suggest that groups can develop shared somatic awareness – a collective felt sense of a situation. This is not mystical but practical: when people in a room are simultaneously attending to their embodied experience, they often notice convergent patterns that weren't accessible through verbal exchange alone.

**Metaphor as connective tissue.** The ZRM's use of images and figurative language creates shared symbolic spaces. When a group collectively develops metaphors for their situation and aspirations ("We are mycelium, connecting invisible underground networks"), these function as what Fabric's mission calls "memes of interconnectedness" – they carry embodied, relational meaning that analytical statements cannot.

**Neural network as metaphor for actual network.** The ZRM defines resources as "neuronal excitation patterns that have a supportive effect on the intentions that clients pursue." This is remarkably parallel to Fabric's understanding of its own structure: a network where knowledge and capacity reside in *connections and relationships*, not in isolated individuals or organisations. What if the network itself could be understood as having something analogous to somatic markers – embodied signals about its own health, alignment, and direction?

## 5. How This Fits the Fabric Design Scaffolding

### 5.1 Points of Alignment

The ZRM resonates with several principles and mechanisms already in the design scaffolding:

**Living System 2 (Collective Decision Making)**: The scaffolding asks "Who decides what, and how?" The ZRM suggests an additional question: "*With what quality of attention* do we decide?" Adding somatic sensing to consent processes could deepen the quality of decisions without slowing them down – a brief somatic check-in ("Notice your body's response to this proposal") takes seconds but adds a whole dimension of information.

**Living System 3 (Feedback & Learning)**: Principle 3.2 ("Honour relational knowing; resist extractive metrics") maps directly onto the ZRM's insistence that body-based knowing is a valid and essential source of information. The ZRM's affect balance technique – checking the somatic valence of experiences and plans – could be adapted as a feedback practice: not just "What did we learn?" but "How does our body respond when we sit with what happened?"

**Convergent Facilitation**: Kashtan's approach of identifying underlying needs rather than surface positions is deeply compatible with ZRM. Somatic markers can help identify genuine needs more reliably than verbal articulation alone – the body often knows what matters before the mind can name it.

**Conflict as information**: The Cynefin tradition and the design scaffolding both reframe conflict as carrying information about system dynamics. Somatic awareness adds texture to this: what does the conflict feel like in the room? Where is energy concentrated? What does collective tension reveal about what's at stake?

### 5.2 What ZRM Adds That's Currently Missing

The design scaffolding is comprehensive on *structural* mechanisms (consent processes, decision rights, escalation protocols) but relatively thin on the *experiential and embodied dimensions* of decision-making. The ZRM addresses a gap that the scaffolding currently flags but doesn't fill:

1. **The agency gap**: The scaffolding designs good processes for making decisions but doesn't directly address whether those decisions get enacted with full energy. The ZRM's focus on the intention-behaviour gap is exactly the missing piece.

2. **Somatic dimension of consent**: "Safe enough to try" is a cognitive assessment. The ZRM suggests a parallel somatic assessment: "Does my body register safety when I sit with this?" This isn't a replacement but a complement – enriching rational consent with embodied consent.

3. **Embodied practices as infrastructure**: The mission document mentions "embodied practices" and "embodied experiences of interconnectedness" but the scaffolding doesn't yet specify what these look like in practice. The ZRM provides a concrete toolkit: image work, somatic tracking, embodiment exercises, environmental priming.

4. **A bridge between individual and collective agency**: The ZRM's individual self-management process could be adapted as a template for *collective* self-management – the network developing its own "motto goal," checking its collective somatic response to strategic options, building shared embodiment practices into its rhythm.

### 5.3 Tensions and Risks

**Individualisation risk.** The ZRM is an individual self-management method. Applying it to collective decision-making requires careful adaptation to avoid reducing collective processes to aggregated individual experiences. The risk is that "check your somatic response" becomes a new form of individualism dressed in body language. The adaptation must be genuinely relational – somatic *resonance between* people, not just somatic *inventory of* individuals.

**Cultural accessibility.** Somatic literacy is unevenly distributed. Trauma, neurodivergence, cultural background, and embodied experience of oppression all shape people's relationship to body awareness. Any integration of somatic practices must be voluntary, pluralistic, and sensitive to these differences. Some people will connect through movement, others through image, others through verbal metaphor – the ZRM's multi-modal approach is helpful here but needs to be offered as invitation, not requirement.

**Empirical modesty.** The ZRM's evidence base, while promising, is not yet robust. It should be introduced as a resource to experiment with, not as a proven solution – which, given Fabric's commitment to safe-to-fail experimentation, is already the appropriate frame.

**The McGilchrist caveat.** While the "right hemisphere" framing is poetically compelling and practically useful, the neuroscientific evidence for strong hemispheric lateralisation is weaker than McGilchrist claims (Spezio, 2019). It may be more defensible to speak of "broad, contextual, embodied modes of attention" vs. "narrow, analytical, abstracting modes" without making strong hemispheric claims. The functional distinction holds even if the neuroanatomical mapping is more nuanced than the metaphor suggests.

## 6. Practical Experiments for Fabric

Based on this analysis, several concrete experiments could be piloted:

1. **Somatic consent check**: After a standard consent round on a proposal, add a brief somatic check-in: "Notice your body's response. Raise a hand if you notice significant tension or constriction." This doesn't override consent but surfaces information that might otherwise go underground and emerge later as lack of follow-through or passive resistance.

2. **Image-based strategy sessions**: At the start of a strategic process, invite participants to select images (physical cards or digital) based on somatic response, then explore collective patterns. This accesses the "needs phase" of the Rubikon Process at network level.

3. **Collective motto goals**: Develop Fabric-level and working-group-level motto goals using the ZRM process – figurative, embodied expressions of orientation that speak to the whole person. These could complement (not replace) more analytical strategic statements.

4. **Embodiment in meeting rhythms**: Build brief physical practices into regular meeting structures – movement, breathing, body scans – not as warm-ups but as integral information-gathering. What does the group's body know that hasn't been said?

5. **Somatic literacy training for facilitators**: Offer ZRM-informed training as part of the Facilitator role development, building capacity to notice and work with embodied group dynamics.

6. **If-then plans for network commitments**: After collective decisions, support participants in forming specific implementation intentions: "When [situation X occurs], I/we will [do Y]." This creates the strategic automaticity that bridges intention and action.

## 7. Synthesis: Where This All Connects

The deepest connection between the ZRM, McGilchrist, and Fabric's mission lies in their shared diagnosis: the dominant paradigm has over-invested in one mode of knowing (analytical, abstract, disembodied, separating) at the expense of another (embodied, relational, contextual, connecting). All three point toward the same remedy, though from different angles:

- **McGilchrist** provides the philosophical and cultural analysis: we have let the "emissary" (focused, analytical attention) usurp the "master" (broad, embodied, relational attention), creating a world of fragmented abstractions that has lost touch with lived reality.

- **Fabric's mission** provides the political and practical framing: systems of separation enable domination and control; re-establishing interconnectedness is both the ultimate goal and the means of getting there.

- **The ZRM** provides *practical techniques* for making the shift: concrete, learnable methods for accessing embodied knowing, bridging intention and action, and synchronising the analytical mind with the body's deeper intelligence.

The missing piece – and the creative challenge – is extending these individual techniques into genuinely collective practices that serve Fabric's purpose of building collective agency. This is not simply a matter of having everyone in a room do individual somatic check-ins simultaneously. It requires developing what we might call *collective somatic intelligence* – the network's capacity to sense, through embodied relational awareness, what is alive, what is needed, and what wants to happen next.

This is speculative territory, but it is also exactly the kind of "safe-to-fail experiment" that the design scaffolding recommends for complex domains. The question is not "Will this definitely work?" but "Is it safe enough to try, and can we learn from trying?"
